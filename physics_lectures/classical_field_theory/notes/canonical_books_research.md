# Canonical books and paraphrased contents for Classical Field Theory

Purpose: collect useful source books and extract a working order of topics for building our own lecture notes on Classical Field Theory.

This is a planning file. It is meant to help decide chapter order, prerequisites, examples, and optional advanced branches.

The lists below are **paraphrased working tables of contents**. They are not meant to reproduce book front matter typographically. They are written as planning notes: what topic appears, roughly in what order, and why it matters for our course.

---

# 1. L. D. Landau and E. M. Lifshitz — *The Classical Theory of Fields*

## Role

The compact relativistic model: special relativity, covariant electrodynamics, relativistic mechanics, and general relativity treated as field theory.

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

1. Geometry of special relativity: events, intervals, Lorentz transformations, proper time.
2. Relativistic particle mechanics: four-velocity, four-momentum, energy, action for a free particle.
3. Charged particles: interaction term in the action, equations of motion in an electromagnetic field.
4. Electromagnetic field variables: potentials, field tensor, gauge freedom.
5. Maxwell equations in covariant notation: homogeneous and inhomogeneous equations.
6. Conservation laws in electrodynamics: energy, momentum, field stress, field invariants.
7. Radiation from moving charges: retarded potentials, emitted power, radiation reaction themes.
8. Relativistic motion in external electromagnetic fields.
9. Transition from special relativity to curved spacetime.
10. Gravitational field as geometry: metric tensor, connection, curvature.
11. Einstein equations and the action principle for gravity.
12. Motion in gravitational fields: geodesics, weak field, Newtonian limit.
13. Exact and approximate gravitational fields.
14. Cosmological models and large-scale gravitational fields.

## Use for our notes

Use this as the high-level model for the covariant spine of the course.

## Links

- Course overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Course_of_Theoretical_Physics
- Classical field theory overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field_theory

# 2. Davison E. Soper — *Classical Field Theory*

## Role

Probably the closest direct template for a focused classical field theory course: fields, action, symmetries, Noether theorem, electromagnetic and gauge examples.

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

1. Relativistic notation and spacetime conventions.
2. Review of Lagrangian mechanics for finitely many coordinates.
3. Hamilton's principle and variational derivatives.
4. Continuous systems and the passage from particles to fields.
5. Lagrangian density and field equations.
6. Euler--Lagrange equations for scalar fields.
7. Boundary terms and surface terms in field variations.
8. Canonical momenta and Hamiltonian density for fields.
9. Translation invariance and energy-momentum conservation.
10. Rotational and Lorentz invariance.
11. Noether currents for global continuous symmetries.
12. Electromagnetic field as a relativistic field.
13. Potentials, field strength, and gauge transformations.
14. Coupling of fields to particles or sources.
15. Stress-energy tensor and conserved quantities.
16. Gauge symmetry and constraints.
17. Nonlinear classical field equations as optional extensions.

## Use for our notes

Use this as the primary structural reference for Chapters 1--6.

## Links

- Author overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davison_Soper

# 3. Herbert Goldstein, Charles Poole, John Safko — *Classical Mechanics*

## Role

The main mechanics prerequisite: variational mechanics, Hamiltonian formalism, canonical transformations, relativity, and the bridge from mechanics to fields.

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

1. Elementary principles: Newtonian mechanics, constraints, generalized coordinates.
2. Variational principle and Lagrange equations.
3. Central-force motion and effective potentials.
4. Rigid-body kinematics.
5. Rigid-body dynamics.
6. Oscillations and normal modes.
7. Special relativity in classical mechanics.
8. Hamilton equations.
9. Canonical transformations.
10. Hamilton--Jacobi theory.
11. Perturbation theory and action-angle methods.
12. Nonlinear dynamics and chaos.
13. Continua and classical fields as extensions of analytical mechanics.
14. Relativistic field-theory themes and electromagnetic examples.
15. Group-theoretic appendix material useful for symmetry language.

## Use for our notes

The first chapter of our course should explicitly convert the finite-dimensional formalism of this book into field language.

## Links

- Overview and table-of-contents source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Mechanics_(Goldstein)

# 4. J. D. Jackson — *Classical Electrodynamics*

## Role

The canonical graduate electrodynamics source. Maxwell theory is the central classical field theory example.

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

1. Electrostatics: fields, potentials, Gauss law, basic boundary problems.
2. Green functions and boundary-value methods.
3. Multipole expansions and asymptotic fields.
4. Dielectrics and macroscopic electric response.
5. Magnetostatics, vector potential, magnetic materials.
6. Faraday induction and quasi-static approximations.
7. Maxwell equations and local conservation laws.
8. Field energy, field momentum, Poynting theorem, stress tensor.
9. Plane waves in vacuum and media.
10. Propagation, reflection, refraction, dispersion, absorption.
11. Guided waves: waveguides, cavities, fibers.
12. Radiation from localized sources.
13. Multipole radiation.
14. Scattering and diffraction.
15. Special relativity and Lorentz transformations.
16. Covariant electrodynamics.
17. Relativistic particle dynamics in fields.
18. Energy loss, scattering by matter, Cherenkov and transition radiation.
19. Radiation by accelerated charges.
20. Bremsstrahlung and equivalent-photon methods.
21. Radiation damping and classical models of the electron.
22. Units, dimensions, and conventions.

## Use for our notes

Use Jackson for the electrodynamics block: field tensor, stress tensor, conservation laws, radiation, and relativistic charged particles.

## Links

- Overview and table-of-contents source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Electrodynamics_(book)

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

3. Electrostatic potential and energy.
4. Conductors, dielectrics, and polarization.
5. Currents and magnetic fields.
6. Vector potential and magnetic flux.
7. Induction and changing fields.
8. Maxwell equations in physical form.
9. Conservation of charge and local field equations.
10. Energy and momentum stored in fields.
11. Electromagnetic waves.
12. Radiation and antenna-like systems.
13. Fields in matter.
14. Boundary behavior of fields.
15. Relativity of electric and magnetic fields.
16. Field viewpoint as a bridge to quantum theory.

## Use for our notes

# 6. David Bleecker — *Gauge Theory and Variational Principles*

Mathematical gauge theory from variational principles: bundles, connections, curvature, gauge transformations, Yang--Mills theory.

## Paraphrased contents / topic order

1. Variational calculus and field equations.
2. Differential forms and exterior calculus.
3. Manifolds as configuration spaces for fields.
4. Fiber bundles and local trivializations.
5. Principal bundles and structure groups.
6. Connections as gauge potentials.
7. Curvature as field strength.
8. Gauge transformations.
9. Covariant derivatives.
10. Electromagnetism as an Abelian gauge theory.
11. Yang--Mills fields as non-Abelian generalization.
12. Lagrangians built from curvature.
13. Noether identities and gauge redundancy.
14. Coupling to matter fields.
15. Geometric interpretation of conservation laws.
16. Variational derivation of gauge-field equations.

## Use for our notes
