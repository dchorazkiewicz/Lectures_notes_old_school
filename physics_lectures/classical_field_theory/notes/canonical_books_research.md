# Canonical books and structural map for Classical Field Theory

Purpose: collect canonical references and extract a working order of topics for building our own lecture notes on Classical Field Theory.

This is a planning file, not teaching text. It is meant to help decide chapter order, prerequisites, examples, and optional advanced branches.

## Core rule for using this file

Use the books as structural references, not as text to copy. The useful object here is the **topic sequence**: what comes before what, and which themes are repeated across the classical-field-theory tradition.

---

## 1. L. D. Landau and E. M. Lifshitz — *The Classical Theory of Fields*

**Role in our project:** compact relativistic model of field theory; good reference for special relativity, covariant electromagnetism, and gravitation.

**Useful structural sequence:**

1. Special relativity and four-dimensional notation.
2. Relativistic mechanics of particles.
3. Variational principles for relativistic motion.
4. Electromagnetic field tensor and Maxwell equations.
5. Charged particles in electromagnetic fields.
6. Radiation and relativistic electrodynamics.
7. Metric field and gravitational field equations.
8. General relativity as a classical field theory.
9. Exact solutions and cosmological applications.

**Use in our course:** after the introductory variational chapter, use Landau--Lifshitz style notation to keep the course compact and covariant.

**Links:**

- Course overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Course_of_Theoretical_Physics
- Classical field theory overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field_theory

## 2. Davison E. Soper — *Classical Field Theory*

**Role in our project:** direct source for a concise field-theory course built from Lagrangians and symmetries.

**Useful structural sequence:**

1. Special relativity and tensor notation.
2. Action principles for systems with finitely many degrees of freedom.
3. Fields as infinitely many degrees of freedom.
4. Lagrangian densities and Euler--Lagrange field equations.
5. Symmetries and Noether currents.
6. Scalar fields and wave equations.
7. Electromagnetic field as a gauge field.
8. Stress-energy tensor.
9. Relativistic particle-field interaction.
10. Classical gauge-field examples.

**Use in our course:** this is probably the closest model for the first clean version of our own lecture notes.

**Links:**

- Author overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davison_Soper

## 3. Herbert Goldstein, Charles Poole, John Safko — *Classical Mechanics*

**Role in our project:** prerequisite architecture: Lagrangian mechanics, Hamiltonian mechanics, symmetries, variational calculus.

**Useful structural sequence:**

1. Generalized coordinates and constraints.
2. Hamilton's principle.
3. Lagrange equations.
4. Conservation laws and symmetry.
5. Small oscillations and normal modes.
6. Special relativity in mechanics.
7. Hamilton equations.
8. Canonical transformations.
9. Hamilton--Jacobi theory.
10. Transition from discrete systems to continua and fields.

**Use in our course:** build the opening chapter from mechanics to fields: finite-dimensional action first, field action second.

**Links:**

- Overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Mechanics_(Goldstein)
