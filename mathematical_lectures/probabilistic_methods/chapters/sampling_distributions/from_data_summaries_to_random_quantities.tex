\section{From data summaries to random quantities}

The previous chapter studied an observed dataset
\[
x_1,\ldots,x_n.
\]
After observation, its mean, variance, median, and empirical CDF are fixed
summaries. Before observation, the entries are random variables
\[
X_1,\ldots,X_n,
\]
so a summary calculated from them is also random.

\begin{definitionbox}
A \textbf{statistic} is a function of a random sample:
\[
T=g(X_1,\ldots,X_n).
\]
For observed values \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\), the same rule gives the realized value
\[
t=g(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
\]
\end{definitionbox}

For the sample mean,
\[
\overline X=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^nX_i
\]
is a random variable, while
\[
\bar x=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i
\]
is one observed number.

\subsection*{Sampling variation}

Another sample from the same probability model generally produces another value
of the statistic. Statistical reasoning therefore requires a description of this
sample-to-sample variation.

\begin{definitionbox}
The \textbf{sampling distribution} of \(T\) is the probability law of
\[
T=g(X_1,\ldots,X_n)
\]
under the specified sampling model.
\end{definitionbox}

\subsection*{Two distributions}

The empirical distribution describes the values inside one observed dataset. A
sampling distribution describes possible values of a statistic across possible
samples.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Object & Level & Main question \\
\midrule
Empirical distribution & observed data & How are the observations distributed? \\
Sampling distribution & random sampling & How does the statistic vary? \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The next section studies the complete random sample. A later section then obtains
the sampling distribution by applying a statistic to every possible sample.

\begin{theorembox}
The central transition is
\[
\text{observed data}\longrightarrow\text{fixed summaries}
\]
and then
\[
\text{random samples}\longrightarrow\text{random summaries}.
\]
\end{theorembox}
