\section{Final checklist}

The essential chain of statistical inference is
\[
\text{model}
\longrightarrow
\text{sampling distribution}
\longrightarrow
\text{large-sample regularity}
\longrightarrow
\text{observed statistic}
\longrightarrow
\text{conclusion}.
\]
The conclusion depends on the sampling mechanism and assumptions, not on the
observed number alone.

\subsection*{Limit theorems}

For independent identically distributed observations with mean \(\mu\) and finite
variance \(\sigma^2\),
\[
\overline X_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu.
\]
This finite-variance law of large numbers explains stabilization. More general
versions require only a finite expectation.

If \(0<\sigma^2<\infty\), the central limit theorem gives
\[
\frac{\overline X_n-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}
\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1).
\]
The law of large numbers identifies where the statistic stabilizes. The central
limit theorem describes the scale and approximate shape of its remaining
fluctuations.

\subsection*{Standard errors}

The standard error is the standard deviation of a sampling distribution:
\[
\operatorname{SE}(T)=\sqrt{\Var(T)}.
\]
For a sample mean and a sample proportion,
\[
\operatorname{SE}(\overline X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n},
\qquad
\operatorname{SE}(\widehat p)=\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}.
\]
Unknown quantities are often replaced by estimates. For the mean,
\[
S^2=\frac1{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\overline X)^2
\]
is used because \(\E[S^2]=\sigma^2\) under independent identical sampling with
finite variance.

\subsection*{Confidence intervals}

A large-sample confidence interval often has the form
\[
\text{estimate}
\pm
\text{critical value}\times\text{estimated standard error}.
\]
The confidence level is the long-run coverage of the interval-producing
procedure. Direct inversion is straightforward when the standard error is known
and does not depend on the unknown parameter. A plug-in standard error creates an
additional approximation.

\subsection*{Hypothesis tests}

A test specifies both
\[
H_0:\theta=\theta_0
\]
and an alternative such as
\[
H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0,
\qquad
H_1:\theta>\theta_0,
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
H_1:\theta<\theta_0.
\]
The alternative determines the relevant tail or tails of the null distribution.

A standardized statistic often has the form
\[
Z=\frac{T-\theta_0}{\operatorname{SE}_{H_0}(T)}.
\]
The p-value is the probability under \(H_0\) of a result at least as extreme as
the observed result in the direction specified by \(H_1\). It is not
\[
P(H_0\text{ is true}\mid\text{data}).
\]
Failure to reject \(H_0\) is not proof that it is true.

\subsection*{Typical tasks}

You should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item distinguish the law of large numbers from the central limit theorem;
    \item calculate and interpret standard errors;
    \item explain why corrected sample variance uses \(n-1\);
    \item construct and interpret a basic large-sample confidence interval;
    \item identify when a standard error has been estimated rather than known;
    \item state both a null and an alternative hypothesis;
    \item select the correct tail or tails from the alternative;
    \item interpret a p-value as a probability about hypothetical data under the
    null model;
    \item distinguish statistical detectability from practical importance;
    \item identify the assumptions supporting an inferential conclusion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Common mistakes}

Avoid:
\begin{itemize}
    \item claiming that a large-sample average becomes exactly equal to its mean;
    \item claiming that the central limit theorem makes the population normal;
    \item confusing population standard deviation with standard error;
    \item treating an estimated standard error as if it were known exactly;
    \item interpreting confidence level as posterior probability for a fixed
    parameter;
    \item defining a p-value without specifying the alternative direction;
    \item treating a large p-value as proof of the null hypothesis;
    \item treating statistical significance as practical importance;
    \item believing that a large sample repairs bias or an incorrect model.
\end{itemize}

\begin{summarybox}
Inference is possible because statistics have predictable sampling behaviour.
Limit theorems provide stability and approximate shape, standard errors provide a
scale, and interval and testing procedures connect these regularities with one
observed sample while keeping assumptions explicit.
\end{summarybox}
