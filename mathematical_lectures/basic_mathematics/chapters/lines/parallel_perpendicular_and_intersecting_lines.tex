\section{Parallel, perpendicular, and intersecting lines}

Once we can describe one line, we can compare two lines. Direction vectors make parallelism natural. Dot products make perpendicularity natural. Implicit equations make intersections natural because they become systems of linear equations.

\subsection*{Parallel lines from direction vectors}

Two lines are parallel when their non-zero direction vectors are scalar multiples of one another.

\begin{definitionbox}
Let \(\ell_1\) and \(\ell_2\) have direction vectors \(v_1\) and \(v_2\). They are parallel if there exists a non-zero scalar \(\lambda\) such that
\[
v_2=\lambda v_1.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
\ell_1:P=(1,2)+t(2,-3)
\]
and
\[
\ell_2:P=(-4,0)+s(-4,6),
\]
we have \((-4,6)=-2(2,-3)\), so the lines are parallel.
\end{examplebox}

Parallel lines may be distinct or may describe the same line. If they share a point, they are the same line; otherwise they are distinct parallel lines.

\begin{examplebox}
The lines
\[
\ell_1:P=(1,2)+t(2,-3)
\]
and
\[
\ell_2:P=(3,-1)+s(4,-6)
\]
have parallel directions. Moreover, \((3,-1)\) is obtained from \(\ell_1\) when \(t=1\). Therefore the two parametrizations describe the same line.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection*{Perpendicular lines}

In the plane, two non-parallel lines automatically intersect. In space, position must also be checked: skew lines can have perpendicular direction vectors without meeting.

\begin{definitionbox}
Let \(\ell_1\) and \(\ell_2\) be intersecting lines with non-zero direction vectors \(v_1\) and \(v_2\). The lines are perpendicular if
\[
v_1\cdot v_2=0.
\]
For lines in the plane, perpendicular direction vectors already guarantee that the lines intersect.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{examplebox}
Let two lines in the plane have directions
\[
v_1=(3,1),
\qquad
v_2=(2,-6).
\]
Then
\[
v_1\cdot v_2=3\cdot2+1\cdot(-6)=0.
\]
The directions are perpendicular, and the plane lines therefore meet at a right angle.
\end{examplebox}

\begin{remarkbox}
For lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), the equation \(v_1\cdot v_2=0\) describes perpendicular directions. To call the lines themselves perpendicular, one must additionally verify that they intersect.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsection*{Using normal vectors in the plane}

For lines
\[
\ell_1:A_1x+B_1y+C_1=0,
\qquad
\ell_2:A_2x+B_2y+C_2=0,
\]
the normal vectors are
\[
n_1=(A_1,B_1),
\qquad
n_2=(A_2,B_2).
\]
The lines are parallel when their normal vectors are parallel. They are perpendicular when their normal vectors are perpendicular.

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
\ell_1:2x-3y+1=0,
\qquad
\ell_2:4x-6y-5=0,
\]
we have
\[
(4,-6)=2(2,-3),
\]
so the lines are parallel.
\end{examplebox}

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
\ell_1:x+2y-4=0,
\qquad
\ell_2:2x-y+3=0,
\]
the normals satisfy
\[
(1,2)\cdot(2,-1)=0.
\]
Therefore the lines are perpendicular.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection*{Intersecting lines as a system of equations}

Two non-parallel lines in the plane intersect in exactly one point. Their intersection is found by solving their equations simultaneously.

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
\ell_1:x+y-3=0,
\qquad
\ell_2:2x-y=0,
\]
we solve
\[
\begin{cases}
x+y=3,\\
2x-y=0.
\end{cases}
\]
Adding the equations gives \(3x=3\), so \(x=1\), and then \(y=2\). The intersection point is
\[
(1,2).
\]
\end{examplebox}

\begin{summarybox}
When comparing two lines, ask:
\begin{itemize}
\item Are the lines in the plane or in space?
\item Are their direction vectors parallel or perpendicular?
\item In space, do lines with perpendicular directions actually intersect?
\item If the lines are parallel, are they identical or distinct?
\item If plane lines are not parallel, what is their intersection point?
\end{itemize}
\end{summarybox}

Direction, position, and dimension must all be read before the geometric relationship is named.
