\section{Basic differentiation rules}

Differentiation rules are shortcuts justified by the limit definition.

\subsection*{Constants and powers}

A constant function has derivative zero. For every real exponent \(p\), the power rule is valid on \(x>0\):
\[
\frac{d}{dx}x^p=px^{p-1}.
\]
For integer exponents, the formula extends to all points where the power is defined. Fractional powers require their real domains to be checked separately.

\begin{examplebox}
We have
\[
\frac{d}{dx}x^5=5x^4,
\]
\[
\frac{d}{dx}x^{-3}=-3x^{-4},
\qquad x\ne0,
\]
and
\[
\frac{d}{dx}\sqrt{x}=\frac1{2\sqrt{x}},
\qquad x>0.
\]
Although \(\sqrt{x}\) is defined at zero, its derivative is not finite there.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection*{Constant multiples, sums, and differences}

For a constant \(c\),
\[
(cf)'=cf'.
\]
For differentiable functions \(f\) and \(g\),
\[
(f+g)'=f'+g',
\qquad
(f-g)'=f'-g'.
\]

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
y=5x^{15}-x^2+\frac13x-2,
\]
we obtain
\[
y'=75x^{14}-2x+\frac13.
\]
\end{examplebox}

\subsection*{Rewrite before differentiating}

Roots and fractions often become powers, but the original domain must remain visible. On \(x>0\),
\[
y=\frac2{\sqrt{x}}-\frac3{\sqrt{x^3}}
=2x^{-1/2}-3x^{-3/2},
\]
so
\[
y'=-x^{-3/2}+\frac92x^{-5/2}.
\]

\begin{summarybox}
Before differentiating, identify the domain, rewrite roots and fractions when useful, and check that every formula is valid on the interval being studied.
\end{summarybox}
