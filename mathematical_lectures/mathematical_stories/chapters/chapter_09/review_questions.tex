\section{Review Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Define a function as an assignment. Distinguish domain, codomain, and range, and explain why a formula alone may not determine the whole function.

\item Compare a formula, a table, and a graph as representations of the same function. What information is especially visible in each representation?

\item For $f(x)=x^2-4$, determine the domain, zeros, sign intervals, symmetry, and visible long-term behaviour. Explain how each property appears on the graph.

\item Compare the qualitative behaviour of a polynomial, $e^x$, and $\sin x$. Focus on zeros, sign, monotonicity, boundedness, and periodicity rather than on detailed calculation.

\item Explain geometrically the transformations
\[
f(x)+c,\qquad f(x-c),\qquad -f(x),\qquad f(-x).
\]
Which affect outputs, which affect inputs, and why do horizontal shifts often appear with the opposite sign?

\item Define composition of functions. For two elementary functions, compute both $f\circ g$ and $g\circ f$, state the relevant domains, and explain why the two compositions need not agree.

\item What does it mean for a function to be one-to-one? Explain why this property is necessary for an inverse function and describe the horizontal-line test.

\item Explain why a sequence is a function with a discrete domain. Compare explicit and recursive descriptions, and give one advantage of each.

\item For an arithmetic sequence and a geometric sequence:
\begin{enumerate}
\item identify the defining pattern;
\item write an explicit formula;
\item describe the qualitative behaviour for representative positive, negative, and fractional parameters.
\end{enumerate}

\item Compare the sequences $1/n$, $n$, and $(-1)^n$ in terms of monotonicity and boundedness. Which properties prepare the later study of convergence, and why is neither monotonicity nor boundedness alone sufficient?
\end{enumerate}
