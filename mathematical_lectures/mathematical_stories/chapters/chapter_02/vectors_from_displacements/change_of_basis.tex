\subsection{Changing the Basis: New Coordinates for the Same Vector}

A vector is a geometric displacement. Its coordinate column depends on the
chosen basis. Changing the basis does not rotate or deform the vector itself;
it changes only the pair of numbers used to describe it.

\subsubsection*{What a basis is}

\begin{definitionbox}
Two vectors $\mathbf b_1$ and $\mathbf b_2$ form a basis of the plane when they
are not parallel. Equivalently, every vector $\mathbf u$ can then be written in
one and only one way as
\[
\boxed{\mathbf u=c_1\mathbf b_1+c_2\mathbf b_2}.
\]
The coordinate column of $\mathbf u$ in this basis is
\[
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
=
\begin{pmatrix}c_1\\c_2\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathcal B=(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2).
\]
\end{definitionbox}

The vectors $\mathbf b_1$ and $\mathbf b_2$ need not be perpendicular and need
not have unit length. They only need to point in different directions.

\subsubsection*{The standard basis and a slanted basis}

The standard basis is
\[
\mathcal E=(\mathbf e_1,\mathbf e_2),
\qquad
\mathbf e_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf e_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
For this basis,
\[
\mathbf u=x\mathbf e_1+y\mathbf e_2
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}
=
\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Now choose the slanted basis
\[
\mathbf b_1=\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf b_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The same vector
\[
\mathbf u=\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}
\]
has standard coordinates $(5,4)$, but in the slanted basis we solve
\[
\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}
=
c_1\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}
+c_2\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}.
\]
This gives
\[
2c_1+c_2=5,
\qquad
c_1+2c_2=4,
\]
so
\[
c_1=2,
\qquad
c_2=1.
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}
}.
\]

The vector has not changed. Only the coordinate grid used to count its
components has changed from rectangular to slanted.

\subsubsection*{The basis matrix}

Place the basis vectors as columns of a matrix:
\[
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
|&|\\
\mathbf b_1&\mathbf b_2\\
|&|
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then the equation
\[
\mathbf u=c_1\mathbf b_1+c_2\mathbf b_2
\]
becomes
\[
\boxed{
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}=B[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
}.
\]
Because $\mathbf b_1$ and $\mathbf b_2$ are not parallel, $B$ is invertible.
Hence
\[
\boxed{
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}=B^{-1}[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}
}.
\]

For the example,
\[
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
2&1\\
1&2
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B^{-1}=\frac13
\begin{pmatrix}
2&-1\\
-1&2
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Therefore
\[
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
=
\frac13
\begin{pmatrix}
2&-1\\
-1&2
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\subsubsection*{Changing directly from one basis to another}

Let
\[
\mathcal B=(\mathbf b_1,\mathbf b_2),
\qquad
\mathcal C=(\mathbf c_1,\mathbf c_2),
\]
and let $B$ and $C$ be their basis matrices in standard coordinates. Since
\[
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}
=B[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
=C[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal C},
\]
we obtain
\[
\boxed{
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal C}=C^{-1}B[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
}.
\]
The matrix
\[
P_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}=C^{-1}B
\]
is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal B$-coordinates to
$\mathcal C$-coordinates.

\begin{remarkbox}
The columns of $P_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}$ are the
$\mathcal C$-coordinates of the old basis vectors $\mathbf b_1$ and
$\mathbf b_2$. This gives a direct geometric interpretation of every column.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsubsection*{Why ordinary perpendicular projections fail for a slanted basis}

For an orthonormal basis $(\mathbf e_1,\mathbf e_2)$, the coordinates are
\[
x=\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf e_1,
\qquad
y=\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf e_2.
\]
This works because the basis vectors are perpendicular unit vectors.

For a slanted basis, however,
\[
c_1\neq\frac{\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf b_1}{\mathbf b_1\cdot\mathbf b_1}
\quad\text{and generally}\quad
c_2\neq\frac{\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf b_2}{\mathbf b_2\cdot\mathbf b_2}.
\]
Those expressions are perpendicular projections onto the two basis directions,
but the two projected pieces generally do not add back to $\mathbf u$.

There is nevertheless a projection-like way to read the coefficients. One uses
the \emph{dual basis} $(\mathbf b^1,\mathbf b^2)$, defined by
\[
\mathbf b^i\cdot\mathbf b_j=\delta^i_j.
\]
Then
\[
\boxed{
c_1=\mathbf b^1\cdot\mathbf u,
\qquad
c_2=\mathbf b^2\cdot\mathbf u
}.
\]
Thus ordinary dot products extract coordinates only after the correct dual
directions have been constructed. Algebraically, the rows of $B^{-1}$ are the
transpose coordinates of the dual basis vectors.

\begin{summarybox}
A change of basis changes coordinates, not the geometric vector:
\[
\mathbf u
\quad\text{is fixed, while}\quad
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}
\quad\text{depends on }\mathcal B.
\]
For a basis matrix $B$,
\[
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}=B[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B},
\qquad
[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal B}=B^{-1}[\mathbf u]_{\mathcal E}.
\]
Rectangular coordinates are a special case coming from an orthonormal basis.
Slanted coordinates use the same principle, but their coefficients are found
from a linear system rather than from ordinary perpendicular projections onto
the basis vectors.
\end{summarybox}
