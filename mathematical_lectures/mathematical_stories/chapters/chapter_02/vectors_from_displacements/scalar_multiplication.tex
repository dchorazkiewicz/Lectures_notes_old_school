\subsection{Parallel Vectors and Multiplication by a Number}

Before multiplying a vector by a number, we identify the geometric relation that
this operation is meant to preserve.

Two nonzero free vectors $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$ have the same direction and
the same orientation when placing them successively produces no shortening:
\[
|\mathbf a+\mathbf b|=|\mathbf a|+|\mathbf b|.
\]
They have the same direction and opposite orientations when
\[
|\mathbf a-\mathbf b|=|\mathbf a|+|\mathbf b|.
\]
In both cases the vectors are called \emph{parallel}.

Let
\[
\mathbf a=[a_1,a_2],
\qquad
\mathbf b=[b_1,b_2].
\]
The equality condition in the triangle inequality implies that the two vectors
are parallel exactly when their coordinate pairs are proportional. Thus
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf a\parallel\mathbf b
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\lambda\mathbf a=\mu\mathbf b
}
\]
for some real numbers $\lambda$ and $\mu$ that are not both zero. In the plane,
this condition may also be written as
\[
\boxed{
a_1b_2-a_2b_1=0
}.
\]
If $\lambda$ and $\mu$ have the same sign, the vectors have the same
orientation; if their signs are opposite, the orientations are opposite.

We can now define multiplication of a vector by a real number $t$.

If $t>0$, the vector $t\mathbf a$ is parallel to $\mathbf a$, has the same
orientation, and has length
\[
|t\mathbf a|=t|\mathbf a|.
\]
If $t<0$, it is parallel to $\mathbf a$, has the opposite orientation, and has
length
\[
|t\mathbf a|=|t|\,|\mathbf a|.
\]
For $t=0$, the result is the zero vector.

In coordinates this geometric definition becomes
\[
\boxed{
t[a_1,a_2]=[ta_1,ta_2]
}.
\]

The diagram shows all cases at once:
\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
 t & \text{length} & \text{orientation}\
 \hline
 t>1 & \text{increased} & \text{unchanged}\
 0<t<1 & \text{decreased} & \text{unchanged}\
 t=0 & 0 & \text{none}\
 t<0 & |t|\,|\mathbf a| & \text{reversed}
\end{array}
\]

Multiplication by a number is compatible with vector addition:
\[
\boxed{
t(\mathbf a+\mathbf b)=t\mathbf a+t\mathbf b
}
\]
and
\[
\boxed{
(s+t)\mathbf a=s\mathbf a+t\mathbf a
}.
\]
These identities follow directly from coordinatewise addition and
multiplication, but their geometric content is that scaling commutes with
combining directed changes.
