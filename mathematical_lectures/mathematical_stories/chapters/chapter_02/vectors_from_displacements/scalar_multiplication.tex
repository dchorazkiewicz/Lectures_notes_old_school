\subsection{Stretching, Shrinking, and Reversing a Displacement}

Addition combines different displacements. A second operation appears when the
same displacement is repeated.

If
\[
\mathbf v=(v_1,v_2),
\]
then performing it twice gives
\[
\mathbf v+\mathbf v=(2v_1,2v_2).
\]
Performing it three times gives
\[
3\mathbf v=(3v_1,3v_2).
\]
The coordinates are multiplied by the same number because both components must
be stretched by the same factor if the direction is to remain unchanged.

This suggests the general definition
\[
\boxed{
\lambda\mathbf v=(\lambda v_1,\lambda v_2),
\qquad \lambda\in\mathbb R
}.
\]
The number $\lambda$ is called a scalar. It does not provide a new direction;
it changes how much of the old displacement is taken.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9,>=stealth]
    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (Vhalf) at (1.2,0.7);
    \coordinate (V) at (2.4,1.4);
    \coordinate (TwoV) at (4.8,2.8);
    \coordinate (MinusV) at (-2.4,-1.4);

    \draw[green!55!black,line width=1pt,->] (O) -- (Vhalf)
        node[midway,above left] {$\tfrac12\mathbf v$};
    \draw[blue!70!black,line width=1.1pt,->] (O) -- (V)
        node[midway,below right] {$\mathbf v$};
    \draw[orange!85!black,line width=1.1pt,->] (O) -- (TwoV)
        node[midway,above left] {$2\mathbf v$};
    \draw[violet!75,line width=1.1pt,->] (O) -- (MinusV)
        node[midway,above left] {$-\mathbf v$};

    \draw[gray,dashed] (-3.0,-1.75) -- (5.4,3.15);
    \fill (O) circle (2pt) node[below right] {$O$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The picture separates the possible cases:

\begin{itemize}
    \item if $\lambda>1$, the vector is stretched;
    \item if $0<\lambda<1$, it is shortened;
    \item if $\lambda=0$, it collapses to the zero vector;
    \item if $\lambda<0$, it is also reversed.
\end{itemize}

In every case the result lies on the same line through the origin as
$\mathbf v$. This gives a first geometric meaning of scalar multiples:
nonzero vectors $\mathbf u$ and $\mathbf v$ are parallel precisely when one is
a scalar multiple of the other.

Scalar multiplication also explains why fractions and real factors are natural.
The vector $\tfrac12\mathbf v$ is the displacement that must be repeated twice
to produce $\mathbf v$, while $\sqrt2\,\mathbf v$ has the same direction and
$\sqrt2$ times the length. The operation extends the simple idea of repetition
to continuous scaling.
