\subsection{Anchored and Free Vectors in Cartesian Space}

The Cartesian plane is a set of points described by ordered pairs of real
numbers. Let
\[
P=(p_1,p_2),
\qquad
Q=(q_1,q_2).
\]
The points $P$ and $Q$ determine an ordered pair of points $(P,Q)$. Geometrically,
this ordered pair is represented by an arrow beginning at $P$ and ending at $Q$.
We denote it by
\[
\overrightarrow{PQ}
\]
and call it an \emph{anchored vector}.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-0.5,0) -- (7.0,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-0.5) -- (0,5.2) node[above] {$y$};

    \coordinate (P) at (1.2,1.1);
    \coordinate (Q) at (5.2,4.0);
    \coordinate (R) at (5.2,1.1);

    \draw[helper,dashed] (P) -- (R);
    \draw[helper,dashed] (R) -- (Q);
    \draw[subset,->] (P) -- (Q)
        node[midway,above left] {$\overrightarrow{PQ}$};

    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (P) circle (2pt)
        node[below left] {$P=(p_1,p_2)$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (Q) circle (2pt)
        node[above right] {$Q=(q_1,q_2)$};

    \node[below] at (3.2,1.1) {$q_1-p_1$};
    \node[right,rotate=90] at (5.2,2.55) {$q_2-p_2$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The arrow records the change needed to pass from its initial point to its final
point. Its first component is the horizontal change and its second component is
the vertical change. Therefore
\[
\boxed{
[\overrightarrow{PQ}]
=
Q-P
=
(q_1-p_1,\,q_2-p_2)
}.
\]
The square brackets distinguish the coordinate description of the vector from
the particular arrow drawn between $P$ and $Q$.

The order of the points matters. Reversing the ordered pair reverses both
coordinate changes:
\[
[\overrightarrow{QP}]
=
P-Q
=
-(Q-P).
\]

An anchored vector contains two kinds of information at once. It tells us where
the arrow is drawn, because its initial and final points are specified, and it
tells us what directed change the arrow represents. For many geometric and
physical questions, the place where the arrow is drawn is not important. What
matters is only the change itself.

Consider two anchored vectors
\[
\overrightarrow{PQ}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\overrightarrow{RS}.
\]
They represent the same directed change when their coordinate differences are
the same:
\[
\boxed{
\overrightarrow{PQ}=\overrightarrow{RS}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
Q-P=S-R
}.
\]
Thus equal anchored vectors have the same horizontal and vertical changes, even
when they begin at different points.

A \emph{free vector} is the directed change common to all equal anchored
vectors. It is not tied to one initial point. We may move its arrow parallel to
itself without changing the vector, provided that its direction, orientation,
and length remain unchanged.

In Cartesian coordinates, a free vector is completely described by one ordered
pair of real numbers:
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf v=(v_1,v_2)
}.
\]
This pair means a change of $v_1$ in the first coordinate and a change of $v_2$
in the second coordinate. Every anchored vector satisfying
\[
Q-P=(v_1,v_2)
\]
is a representative of the same free vector $\mathbf v$.

There is a particularly simple representative. Let
\[
O=(0,0)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
V=(v_1,v_2).
\]
Then
\[
[\overrightarrow{OV}]
=
V-O
=
(v_1,v_2).
\]
Hence every free vector can be drawn from the origin to the point whose
coordinates are the components of the vector:
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf v=(v_1,v_2)
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\overrightarrow{OV},
\qquad V=(v_1,v_2)
}.
\]
This does not identify points and vectors as the same kind of object. It gives a
convenient canonical representative of a free vector inside the Cartesian
plane.

The next step is to define operations on these free vectors. Because a free
vector is represented by its coordinate pair, addition, subtraction, and
multiplication by a scalar can be introduced through their geometric meaning and
then written in Cartesian coordinates.
