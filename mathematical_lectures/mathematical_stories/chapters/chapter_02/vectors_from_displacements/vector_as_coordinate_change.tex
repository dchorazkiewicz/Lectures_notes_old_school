\subsection{Points and Anchored Vectors}

The Cartesian plane introduced in the previous chapter is a set of points. Each
point is described by an ordered pair of real numbers. Thus
\[
P=(p_1,p_2),
\qquad
Q=(q_1,q_2)
\]
are two positions in the plane.

A point tells us where something is. To describe a directed change from one
position to another, we must keep track of both the starting point and the
endpoint. The ordered pair of points
\[
(P,Q)
\]
determines the arrow beginning at $P$ and ending at $Q$. We denote it by
\[
\overrightarrow{PQ}
\]
and call it an \emph{anchored vector}.

\end{tikzpicture}

The coordinate description of this arrow is obtained by comparing the endpoint
with the starting point. To move from $P$ to $Q$, the first coordinate must
change by $q_1-p_1$ and the second by $q_2-p_2$. Therefore
\[
\boxed{
\overrightarrow{PQ}
=
(q_1-p_1,\,q_2-p_2)
}.
\]

This is the first place where subtraction of points becomes useful. The
expression
\[
Q-P
\]
does not denote another point of the plane. It denotes the coordinate change
that carries $P$ to $Q$:
\[
\boxed{
\overrightarrow{PQ}=Q-P
}.
\]

The order matters. Reversing the two points reverses the arrow:
\[
\overrightarrow{QP}=P-Q=-\overrightarrow{PQ}.
\]

A particularly important anchored vector begins at the origin
\[
O=(0,0).
\]
For a point
\[
P=(p_1,p_2),
\]
the arrow
\[
\overrightarrow{OP}
\]
is called the \emph{position vector} of $P$. We write
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf r_P=\overrightarrow{OP}=(p_1,p_2)
}.
\]

The point $P$ and the position vector $\mathbf r_P$ have the same coordinates,
but they are not the same kind of object. The point is a position. The vector is
the directed arrow from the chosen origin to that position.

Now let $A$ and $B$ be two positions. Their anchored vector satisfies
\[
\overrightarrow{AB}=B-A.
\]
Since
\[
\mathbf r_A=A-O,
\qquad
\mathbf r_B=B-O,
\]
we obtain
\[
\boxed{
\overrightarrow{AB}
=
\mathbf r_B-\mathbf r_A
}.
\]
Thus a displacement is the difference between the final and initial position
vectors.

For motion depending on time, the same relation will later be written as
\[
\Delta\mathbf r
=
\mathbf r(t_2)-\mathbf r(t_1).
\]

At this stage the vector is still anchored to a particular starting point and
endpoint. The next step is to decide when arrows drawn in different places
represent the same directed change. That will lead from anchored vectors to
free vectors.
