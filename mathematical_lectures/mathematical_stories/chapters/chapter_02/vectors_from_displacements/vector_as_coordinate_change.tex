\subsection{Position Vectors and Displacement Vectors}

We begin with a single point in the Cartesian plane. Let
\[
P=(p_1,p_2).
\]
The arrow drawn from the origin $O=(0,0)$ to the point $P$ is called the
\emph{position vector} of $P$:
\[
\boxed{\mathbf r_P=\overrightarrow{OP}=(p_1,p_2)}.
\]

The point and its position vector contain the same coordinate information, but
they describe it in two different ways. The point $P$ marks a place in the
plane. The vector $\mathbf r_P$ describes how to reach that place from the
chosen origin: change the first coordinate by $p_1$ and the second by $p_2$.

Each point has its own position vector, always beginning at the same origin.
For example,
\[
\mathbf r_A=(3.2,2.4),\qquad
\mathbf r_B=(-3.4,2.0),\qquad
\mathbf r_C=(-2.3,-2.5),\qquad
\mathbf r_D=(2.8,-2.2).
\]
The signs of the components show in which directions one must move from the
origin, while their magnitudes describe how far the point lies along the two
coordinate directions.

A position vector is therefore tied to the chosen origin. If the origin is
changed, the vector describing the same point also changes.

Only after position vectors have been introduced do we compare two positions.
Let
\[
A=(x_1,y_1),
\qquad
B=(x_2,y_2),
\]
with position vectors
\[
\mathbf r_A=\overrightarrow{OA},
\qquad
\mathbf r_B=\overrightarrow{OB}.
\]
If an object moves from $A$ to $B$, the arrow from the initial point to the
final point is the \emph{displacement vector}:
\[
\Delta\mathbf r=\overrightarrow{AB}.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-0.5,0) -- (7.0,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-0.5) -- (0,5.2) node[above] {$y$};

    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (A) at (2,1.4);
    \coordinate (B) at (5.4,4.0);

    \draw[helper,->] (O) -- (A)
        node[midway,below right] {$\mathbf r_A$};
    \draw[helper,->] (O) -- (B)
        node[midway,above left] {$\mathbf r_B$};
    \draw[subset,->] (A) -- (B)
        node[midway,below right] {$\Delta\mathbf r=\overrightarrow{AB}$};

    \fill (O) circle (2pt) node[below left] {$O$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (A) circle (2pt) node[below right] {$A$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (B) circle (2pt) node[above right] {$B$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The displacement is the change required to turn the initial position vector
into the final position vector:
\[
\mathbf r_A+\Delta\mathbf r=\mathbf r_B.
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{
\Delta\mathbf r
=\overrightarrow{AB}
=\mathbf r_B-\mathbf r_A
}
\]
and, in coordinates,
\[
\boxed{
\overrightarrow{AB}
=(x_2-x_1,\,y_2-y_1)
}.
\]

A position vector describes where an object is relative to the origin. A
displacement vector describes how its position changes. This distinction will
later allow motion to be written as a time-dependent position vector
$\mathbf r(t)$, with displacement between two moments given by
\[
\Delta\mathbf r
=\mathbf r(t_2)-\mathbf r(t_1).
\]
