\subsection{Addition, Opposite Vectors, and Subtraction}

Let $\overrightarrow{PQ}$ represent the free vector
\[
\mathbf a=[a_1,a_2],
\]
and choose a representative $\overrightarrow{QR}$ of
\[
\mathbf b=[b_1,b_2]
\]
whose initial point is the endpoint of the first arrow. The arrow
$\overrightarrow{PR}$ records the combined change.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.0]
    \coordinate (P) at (0.4,0.5);
    \coordinate (Q) at (3.2,1.4);
    \coordinate (R) at (4.7,4.0);

    \draw[subset,->] (P) -- (Q)
        node[midway,below] {$\mathbf a$};
    \draw[subset,->] (Q) -- (R)
        node[midway,right] {$\mathbf b$};
    \draw[very thick,->] (P) -- (R)
        node[midway,above left] {$\mathbf a+\mathbf b$};

    \fill (P) circle (2pt) node[below left] {$P$};
    \fill (Q) circle (2pt) node[below right] {$Q$};
    \fill (R) circle (2pt) node[above right] {$R$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Since
\[
Q=P+(a_1,a_2)
\]
and
\[
R=Q+(b_1,b_2),
\]
we have
\[
R=P+(a_1+b_1,\,a_2+b_2).
\]
Therefore the free vector represented by $\overrightarrow{PR}$ depends only on
$\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$, not on the chosen point $P$. We define
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf a+\mathbf b
=
[\overrightarrow{PR}]
=
[a_1+b_1,\,a_2+b_2]
}.
\]

The same sum can be constructed in the opposite order. Placing a representative
of $\mathbf b$ first and then a representative of $\mathbf a$ gives the same
endpoint. This is the parallelogram rule.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.95]
    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (A) at (3.1,0.9);
    \coordinate (B) at (1.2,2.6);
    \coordinate (S) at (4.3,3.5);

    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (A) node[midway,below] {$\mathbf a$};
    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (B) node[midway,left] {$\mathbf b$};
    \draw[helper,dashed,->] (A) -- (S) node[midway,right] {$\mathbf b$};
    \draw[helper,dashed,->] (B) -- (S) node[midway,above] {$\mathbf a$};
    \draw[very thick,->] (O) -- (S)
        node[midway,above left] {$\mathbf a+\mathbf b$};

    \fill (O) circle (2pt);
    \fill (A) circle (2pt);
    \fill (B) circle (2pt);
    \fill (S) circle (2pt);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Thus
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf a+\mathbf b=\mathbf b+\mathbf a
}.
\]

For every vector
\[
\mathbf a=[a_1,a_2]
\]
there is an \emph{opposite vector}
\[
\boxed{
-\mathbf a=[-a_1,-a_2]
},
\]
characterised by
\[
\mathbf a+(-\mathbf a)=\mathbf 0.
\]
Geometrically it has the same length and direction line as $\mathbf a$, but the
opposite orientation.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.95]
    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (A) at (3.0,1.7);
    \coordinate (M) at (-3.0,-1.7);

    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (A)
        node[midway,above left] {$\mathbf a$};
    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (M)
        node[midway,below right] {$-\mathbf a$};
    \draw[helper,dashed] (M) -- (A);
    \fill (O) circle (2pt) node[below right] {$O$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Subtraction is defined by adding the opposite vector:
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf a-\mathbf b
=
\mathbf a+(-\mathbf b)
=
[a_1-b_1,\,a_2-b_2]
}.
\]
It is the missing vector in the equation
\[
(\mathbf a-\mathbf b)+\mathbf b=\mathbf a.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.0]
    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (B) at (1.25,2.55);
    \coordinate (A) at (4.55,1.05);

    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (B)
        node[pos=0.48,left=5pt] {$\mathbf b$};
    \draw[very thick,->] (B) -- (A)
        node[pos=0.53,above right=5pt] {$\mathbf a-\mathbf b$};
    \draw[subset,->] (O) -- (A)
        node[pos=0.58,below=5pt] {$\mathbf a$};

    \fill (O) circle (2pt) node[below left] {$O$};
    \fill (B) circle (2pt) node[above left] {$B$};
    \fill (A) circle (2pt) node[above right] {$A$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The last diagram also explains the relation between points and vectors. For any
two points $P$ and $Q$,
\[
[\overrightarrow{PQ}]=Q-P.
\]
The subtraction is performed on their coordinate descriptions and produces the
free vector represented by the arrow from $P$ to $Q$.
