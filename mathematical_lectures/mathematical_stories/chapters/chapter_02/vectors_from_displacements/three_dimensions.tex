\subsection{From the Plane to Three-Dimensional Space}

Nothing essential changes when a third coordinate direction is added. A vector
in space records three coordinate changes:
\[
\mathbf v=(v_1,v_2,v_3).
\]
Addition and scalar multiplication remain componentwise because successive
changes in each coordinate still add independently.

To find the length, first combine the changes in the first two coordinates and
then include the third. Applying the Pythagorean theorem twice gives
\[
\boxed{
\|\mathbf v\|
=
\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}
}.
\]
The dot product extends in the same way:
\[
\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v
=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+u_3v_3.
\]
It still measures the cosine of the angle and still detects perpendicularity.

\subsubsection*{A new geometric question}

In the plane, one nonzero vector determines a line and two nonparallel vectors
already determine the whole plane. In three-dimensional space, two nonparallel
vectors determine a plane through the origin. We can therefore ask:

\begin{center}
\emph{Can we construct a vector perpendicular to that entire plane?}
\end{center}

Such a vector must be perpendicular to both given vectors. If
\[
\mathbf u=(u_1,u_2,u_3),
\qquad
\mathbf v=(v_1,v_2,v_3),
\]
and
\[
\mathbf n=(n_1,n_2,n_3),
\]
then the two perpendicularity conditions are
\[
\begin{aligned}
n_1u_1+n_2u_2+n_3u_3&=0,\\
n_1v_1+n_2v_2+n_3v_3&=0.
\end{aligned}
\]
They describe one line of possible perpendicular vectors. The two opposite
directions on that line are both geometrically perpendicular to the plane.

\subsubsection*{Choosing one of the two perpendicular directions}

The two possible perpendicular vectors differ only by sign. To choose one
consistently, we use the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand
from $\mathbf u$ toward $\mathbf v$ through the smaller angle; the thumb points
in the direction assigned to $\mathbf u\times\mathbf v$.

Reversing the order reverses the chosen direction:
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf v\times\mathbf u
=-(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)
}.
\]
Thus the cross product remembers orientation, unlike the ordinary angle between
vectors.

\subsubsection*{Deriving the coordinate formula}

A convenient solution of the two perpendicularity equations is
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf u\times\mathbf v
=
\bigl(
 u_2v_3-u_3v_2,
 u_3v_1-u_1v_3,
 u_1v_2-u_2v_1
\bigr)
}.
\]
This formula is not accepted without verification. Substitution gives
\[
(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf u=0,
\qquad
(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf v=0,
\]
so the resulting vector is indeed perpendicular to both inputs.

\subsubsection*{Its length is an area}

Let $\theta\in[0,\pi]$ be the angle between $\mathbf u$ and $\mathbf v$. The
parallelogram built on these vectors has base $\|\mathbf u\|$ and height
$\|\mathbf v\|\sin\theta$. Its area is therefore
\[
\|\mathbf u\|\,\|\mathbf v\|\sin\theta.
\]
The cross product is chosen so that its length equals this area:
\[
\boxed{
\|\mathbf u\times\mathbf v\|
=
\|\mathbf u\|\,\|\mathbf v\|\sin\theta
}.
\]
This explains two important cases immediately:
\[
\mathbf u\parallel\mathbf v
\iff
\mathbf u\times\mathbf v=\mathbf0,
\]
because the parallelogram then has zero height, while perpendicular vectors
produce the largest possible area for fixed lengths.

\begin{remarkbox}
The cross product is special to three-dimensional Euclidean space. It combines
three pieces of information: the plane determined by the two vectors, an
orientation of that plane, and the area of their parallelogram.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{summarybox}
The vector chapter now follows one continuous chain of ideas:
\[
\text{displacement}
\longrightarrow
\text{addition and scaling}
\longrightarrow
\text{linear combinations}
\longrightarrow
\text{length and direction}
\longrightarrow
\text{dot product and angle}
\longrightarrow
\text{projection and orthogonal decomposition}.
\]
In three dimensions the same language leads naturally to a vector perpendicular
to a plane and to the cross product, whose length records area.
\end{summarybox}
