\section{Review Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Explain the difference between a point and a vector. Why can the same vector be drawn from different starting points?

\item For points $A=(1,-2)$ and $B=(5,3)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item find the displacement from $A$ to $B$;
\item interpret both components geometrically;
\item find the displacement from $B$ to $A$ and explain the relation between the two vectors.
\end{enumerate}

\item Describe vector addition both geometrically and in coordinates. Why does addition represent the composition of successive displacements?

\item What does scalar multiplication do to the length and direction of a vector? Discuss separately positive, negative, and zero scalars.

\item Explain what a linear combination of two vectors represents. Under what geometric condition can two vectors generate every vector in the plane?

\item For $u=(3,4)$ and $v=(-4,3)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item compute their lengths and scalar product;
\item decide whether they are perpendicular;
\item explain what the scalar product reveals that the coordinate list alone does not immediately show.
\end{enumerate}

\item Derive the formula for the angle between nonzero vectors from the scalar product. What information is lost when one of the vectors is zero?

\item Explain projection as the decomposition of one vector into a component parallel to another vector and a perpendicular remainder. State how the sign of the projection coefficient should be interpreted.

\item What is a basis? Explain carefully the difference between a geometric vector and its coordinate pair relative to a basis. Why may the same vector have different coordinates in two bases?

\item Compare Cartesian and polar coordinates:
\begin{enumerate}
\item what quantities are used in each system;
\item where nonuniqueness appears in polar coordinates;
\item which geometric questions are naturally simplified by the polar description.
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
