\chapter{Integrals and Accumulation}

\begin{corebox}[Elementary techniques, immediate applications]
Integration is developed as the reversal of differentiation and the accumulation of small contributions. The computational toolkit remains deliberately elementary so that area, motion, work, and total change remain visible behind the formulas.
\end{corebox}

\begin{summarybox}[Integration as accumulated change]
Antiderivatives reverse local differentiation. Definite integrals accumulate rates and densities over intervals. Simple calculations are sufficient to connect these ideas with area, displacement, distance in non-negative motion, and work.
\end{summarybox}
