\chapter{Limits and Continuity}

\begin{corebox}[Sequences before function limits]
The idea of a limit is developed first for sequences, where approaching means moving along natural-number indices. Function limits are then introduced through sequences approaching a point, using Heine's theorem and the Cauchy criterion. Only after this foundation do we study computational methods, one-sided behavior, asymptotes, and continuity.
\end{corebox}

\begin{summarybox}[Limits and continuity]
Sequence limits provide the basic definition of convergence, the Cauchy criterion, and the monotone convergence theorem. Heine's theorem transfers this language to functions: a function has a limit precisely when every admissible approaching sequence produces the same output limit. The later techniques and continuity results are applications of this structure, not the starting point.
\end{summarybox}
