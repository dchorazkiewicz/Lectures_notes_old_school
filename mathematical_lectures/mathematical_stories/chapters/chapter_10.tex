\chapter{Limits and Continuity}

\begin{corebox}[Sequences before function limits]
The idea of a limit is developed first for sequences, where approaching means moving along natural-number indices. Function limits are then introduced through sequences approaching a point, using Heine's theorem and the Cauchy criterion. Only after this foundation do we study computational methods, one-sided behaviour, asymptotes, and continuity.
\end{corebox}

\begin{summarybox}[Limits and continuity]
Sequence limits provide the basic language of convergence. Heine's theorem transfers this language to functions: every admissible approaching sequence must produce the same output limit. Cauchy criteria and existence theorems are deeper structural results; algebraic techniques, one-sided limits, asymptotes, and continuity are applications of that structure rather than the starting point.
\end{summarybox}
