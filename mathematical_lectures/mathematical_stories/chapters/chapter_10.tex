\chapter{Limits and Continuity}

\begin{corebox}[Purpose and learning goals]
This chapter makes the idea of approaching a value precise, beginning with sequences and then passing to functions. After completing it, the student should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
\item determine and justify elementary limits of sequences;
\item use Heine's theorem to understand function limits through approaching sequences;
\item recognise one-sided behaviour, continuity, and the main types of discontinuity.
\end{itemize}
\end{corebox}

\begin{remarkbox}[Lecture emphasis]
The live core is sequence convergence, the passage to function limits through Heine's theorem, the $\varepsilon$--$\delta$ picture, one-sided limits, and continuity. Cauchy criteria and existence theorems preserve the deeper argument for careful reading and selective development.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{summarybox}[Limits and continuity]
Sequence limits provide the basic language of convergence. Heine's theorem transfers this language to functions: every admissible approaching sequence must produce the same output limit. Cauchy criteria and existence theorems are deeper structural results; algebraic techniques, one-sided limits, asymptotes, and continuity are applications of that structure rather than the starting point.
\end{summarybox}
