\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item For $f(x)=x^2-1$, compute the average rate of change on $[1,3]$ and $[2,2.5]$. Interpret both as secant slopes.

\item Use the difference quotient to compute the derivative of $f(x)=x^2$ at a general point $a$. Then find the tangent line at $(3,9)$.

\item Differentiate
\[
f(x)=4x^3-3x^2+2x-7,
\qquad
g(x)=3e^x-2\sin x+\cos x.
\]

\item Differentiate
\[
f(x)=x^2e^x,
\qquad
g(x)=\frac{x+1}{x-2},
\qquad
h(x)=(x^2+1)^4.
\]
Name the rule used in each case.

\item Find the tangent and normal lines to $f(x)=x^3-x$ at $x=1$.

\item For $f(x)=x^2-4x+1$, find the critical point and determine where the function increases and decreases.

\item For $f(x)=x^3-3x$, find the critical points and classify the local extrema using a sign table for $f'$.

\item For $f(x)=x^3$, compute $f''$ and determine the intervals of concavity and the inflection point.

\item A particle has position
\[
s(t)=t^2-4t+3.
\]
Find velocity and acceleration, determine when the particle is at rest, and state the sign of the velocity before and after that time.

\item Find the absolute maximum and minimum of
\[
f(x)=x^2-4x+1
\]
on $[0,5]$ by checking the critical point and endpoints.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Development and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item For
\[
f(x)=x^4-4x^2,
\]
perform a complete analysis: compute $f'$ and $f''$, find critical and inflection points, determine monotonicity and concavity intervals, and sketch the graph. Explain how each derivative contributes new information.

\item A particle has
\[
s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t,
\qquad0\le t\le4.
\]
Find velocity and acceleration, intervals of forward and backward motion, rest times, and changes of direction. Interpret every sign change physically.

\item A rectangle has perimeter $20$. Build the area function, identify the physical domain, find and classify the critical point, check endpoint behaviour, and interpret the optimal shape.

\item A closed cylindrical can has fixed volume $V$. Express its surface area using one variable, differentiate, and derive the relation between height and radius at the optimum. State where the modelling assumptions enter.

\item Apply the mean value theorem to $f(x)=x^2$ on $[1,4]$. Find the point $c$, then explain how the theorem converts a finite secant slope into a local tangent slope.

\item Prove, using the mean value theorem, that if $f'(x)>0$ on an interval then $f$ is increasing there. Apply the argument to one concrete function.

\item Give a continuous function with a critical point that is not an extremum. Compute its derivative, analyse the sign, and explain why solving $f'(x)=0$ is insufficient.

\item Compare the local statements supplied by $f'(a)$ and $f''(a)$ with global conclusions about monotonicity and concavity on intervals. Give examples showing why interval information is required.

\item A model produces a mathematically valid critical point outside its physical domain. Construct or analyse such an example and explain why domain checking is part of the mathematics rather than an external correction.

\item Give a complete function-analysis strategy: domain, zeros, limits or end behaviour, first derivative, critical points, monotonicity, extrema, second derivative, concavity, inflection points, sketch, and interpretation. Apply it to a polynomial of degree at most four.
\end{enumerate}
