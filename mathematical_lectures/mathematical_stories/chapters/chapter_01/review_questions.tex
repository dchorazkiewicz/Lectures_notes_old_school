\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary Cartesian exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Plot
\[
A=(3,2),\quad B=(-2,4),\quad C=(-3,-1),\quad D=(2,-3).
\]
For each point, state its quadrant and its signed horizontal and vertical displacement from the origin.

\item For $P=(-4,3)$, draw the orthogonal projections onto both axes, write the projection points, and compute the distances from $P$ to the two axes and to the origin.

\item Compute and illustrate with right triangles the distances
\[
d\bigl((1,2),(4,6)\bigr),
\qquad
d\bigl((-2,1),(4,1)\bigr),
\qquad
d\bigl((-3,-2),(1,1)\bigr).
\]

\item Find the point halfway between $A=(-2,1)$ and $B=(4,5)$. Verify by computing its distance from both endpoints.

\item Prepare a value table for
\[
y=x+2
\]
at $x=-2,-1,0,1,2$. Plot the points, draw the graph, and identify its intersections with both axes.

\item Prepare a value table for
\[
y=-2x+4
\]
at five convenient integer values of $x$. Plot the line and identify its axis intersections.

\item Plot on one coordinate system
\[
y=x+2,
\qquad
y=-2x+4.
\]
Find their intersection algebraically and verify it on the graph.

\item Prepare a table for
\[
y=x^2
\]
at $x=-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3$. Draw the graph and identify its vertex, axis of symmetry, and intersections with the axes.

\item Plot
\[
y=x^2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
y=x+2.
\]
Solve $x^2=x+2$ and mark both intersection points.

\item A point has coordinates $P=(6,-4)$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Find its coordinates when one new unit equals two old units.
\item Find its coordinates when the new origin is the old point $(2,-1)$ and the unit and axis directions remain unchanged.
\item Find its coordinates when only the positive direction of the $x$-axis is reversed.
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Development and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Plot the triangle
\[
A=(-1,1),\qquad B=(5,1),\qquad C=(2,5).
\]
Compute all side lengths, decide whether the triangle is isosceles, and explain how the coordinate differences make the geometry visible.

\item Three consecutive vertices of a rectangle are
\[
A=(-2,-1),\qquad B=(3,-1),\qquad C=(3,2).
\]
Find the fourth vertex, compute the side and diagonal lengths, and justify from coordinates that adjacent sides are perpendicular.

\item Find all points on the $x$-axis whose distance from $P=(2,3)$ is $5$, and all points on the $y$-axis whose distance from $Q=(-4,1)$ is $5$. Plot and verify every answer.

\item In one system a point is $P=(5,3)$. A new origin is placed at the old point $(2,1)$, the $x$-axis direction is reversed, and the unit is unchanged. Compute the new coordinates and describe separately the effect of the translation and the reversed orientation.

\item Plot the line and parabola
\[
y=2x-3,
\qquad
y=x^2-2.
\]
Find their intersection points, check them in both equations, and explain why solving the two equations is the algebraic version of finding common plotted points.

\item Plot
\[
y=x^2-4
\]
and the coordinate axes. Find all axis intersections and use the graph to determine where the function values are positive and where they are negative.

\item Derive
\[
d(P,Q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}
\]
from the horizontal and vertical displacements between the points. Then apply the derivation to one pair of points of your choice.

\item A drawing uses twice the scale horizontally as vertically. Explain why the plotted shape of a circle may look like an ellipse even though the coordinate equation still describes a circle. State what must be adjusted when reading Euclidean lengths from such a drawing.

\item Explain the difference between a geometric point and an ordered pair. Use one fixed point described in two coordinate systems to show what changes and what remains the same.

\item Give a complete Cartesian description of the following process: choose coordinates, plot two elementary graphs, find their common points numerically, and verify each common point in both equations. Explain what the picture contributes and what the calculation proves.
\end{enumerate}
