\section{Review Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item What geometric objects and relations are available before coordinates are introduced? Explain why coordinates describe this geometry rather than create it.

\item State the essential choices required to construct a Cartesian coordinate system. What role is played by the origin, the two perpendicular axes, their orientations, and the common unit length?

\item Describe how orthogonal projection assigns coordinates to a point. Why must the two coordinates be read relative to different axes?

\item Explain the difference between a geometric point and its ordered-pair description. Can the same point receive different coordinates? State precisely what must change for this to happen.

\item Starting from the Pythagorean theorem, derive the distance formula between
\[
P=(x_1,y_1),\qquad Q=(x_2,y_2).
\]
Identify the geometric meaning of each difference appearing in the formula.

\item A point has coordinates $(-3,4)$. Explain what each sign means geometrically, locate the point relative to the axes, and compute its distance from the origin.

\item Distinguish the following statements: a construction postulate, a derived geometric construction, and a numerical formula. Give one example of each from the chapter.

\item Explain why a right angle can be constructed and used before a numerical system of angle measurement is introduced. What additional information does numerical measurement provide?

\item Suppose the common unit on both axes is doubled while the geometric point remains fixed. How do its numerical coordinates change? What geometric quantities remain unchanged?

\item Give a coherent account of the chapter's central transition:
\begin{enumerate}
\item what is known geometrically at the beginning;
\item which choices introduce coordinates;
\item how geometric questions become arithmetic questions;
\item why the final formulas must still be interpreted geometrically.
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
