\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational and graphical exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Plot the points
\[
A=(3,2),\qquad B=(-2,4),\qquad C=(-3,-1),\qquad D=(2,-3).
\]
For each point, state its quadrant and its signed horizontal and vertical displacement from the origin.

\item For the point $P=(-4,3)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item draw its orthogonal projections onto both axes;
\item write the coordinates of both projection points;
\item compute the distance from $P$ to each axis and to the origin.
\end{enumerate}

\item Compute the distances between the following pairs of points and draw the corresponding right triangles:
\[
A=(1,2),\ B=(4,6),
\qquad
C=(-2,1),\ D=(4,1),
\qquad
E=(-3,-2),\ F=(1,1).
\]

\item Find all points on the $x$-axis whose distance from $P=(2,3)$ is $5$. Verify each answer with the distance formula.

\item Find all points on the $y$-axis whose distance from $Q=(-4,1)$ is $5$. Plot the points and verify the answers.

\item A rectangle has three consecutive vertices
\[
A=(-2,-1),\qquad B=(3,-1),\qquad C=(3,2).
\]
Find the fourth vertex, compute all side lengths, and calculate both diagonal lengths.

\item Reflect the point $P=(3,-4)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item across the $x$-axis;
\item across the $y$-axis;
\item across the origin.
\end{enumerate}
Plot the four points and record the coordinate rule used in each reflection.

\item The geometric point $P$ has coordinates $(6,-4)$. The axes and origin remain fixed, but one new coordinate unit equals two old units. Find the new coordinates of $P$. Repeat when one new unit equals one half of an old unit.

\item In the original coordinate system,
\[
P=(5,3),\qquad Q=(-1,4).
\]
A new origin is placed at the old point $O'=(2,1)$ while the axis directions and unit remain unchanged. Find the coordinates of $P$ and $Q$ relative to the point from its coordinate description relative to a chosen system.

\item Explain how orthogonal projection assigns the first and second coordinate of a point. Why are the two coordinates read from different axes?

\item Two Cartesian systems are drawn on the same plane and assign different coordinate pairs to the same point. List the choices that may differ and explain why the geometric point has not changed.

\item Explain what the signs in a coordinate pair record. Why does changing the orientation of one axis change one coordinate sign without moving the point?

\item Derive the distance formula
\[
d(P,Q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}
\]
from the horizontal and vertical coordinate differences. Identify the geometric meaning of every term.

\item A point has coordinates $(4,3)$. The common unit is doubled while the two coordinates of a point. Why is each coordinate read from a different axis?

\item Explain geometrically what the signs in the coordinate pair $(-3,4)$ mean. Why do the signs depend on the chosen positive directions?

\item Starting from the Pythagorean theorem, justify
\[
d(P,Q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}.
\]
Identify the geometric meaning of $x_2-x_1$ and $y_2-y_1$.

\item Why must the horizontal and vertical axes use a common unit for the usual distance formula to represent Euclidean length directly? Describe what changes when the two graphical scales are different.

\item Two students assign different coordinate pairs to the same geometric differences encode geometric distance and direction.
\end{enumerate}
