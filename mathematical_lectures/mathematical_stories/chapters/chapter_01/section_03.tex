\section{Lines in the Cartesian Plane}

A line is one of the simplest geometric subsets of the plane. It can be drawn,
described by a condition on coordinates, or defined explicitly as a subset of
$\mathbb{R}^2$. We will keep these descriptions separate and explain how they
are connected.

\subsection{A Line as a Set of Points}

Consider the condition
\[
y=2x+1.
\]
This is a relation between the coordinates $x$ and $y$. The corresponding
geometric object is the set
\[
L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=2x+1\}.
\]

\begin{definitionbox}
A non-vertical line in the Cartesian plane is a set of the form
\[
L_{m,b}=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=mx+b\},
\]
where $m,b\in\mathbb{R}$ are fixed.
\end{definitionbox}

Every value of $x$ determines exactly one value of $y$. For example, for
$y=2x+1$ we obtain
\[
\begin{array}{c|ccccc}
x&-2&-1&0&1&2\\
\hline
y&-3&-1&1&3&5
\end{array}
\]
and therefore all of the points
\[
(-2,-3),\quad(-1,-1),\quad(0,1),\quad(1,3),\quad(2,5)
\]
belong to $L$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.75]
    \draw[axis] (-3.4,0) -- (3.6,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-4.2) -- (0,6.0) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset,domain=-2.4:2.4,samples=2] plot (\x,{2*\x+1});
    \foreach \x/\y in {-2/-3,-1/-1,0/1,1/3,2/5} {
        \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (\x,\y) circle (2pt);
    }
    \node[subset label,right] at (2.1,5.2) {$L$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{remarkbox}
A finite list of points only gives examples of elements of a line. The complete
line is the entire set of points satisfying its defining condition.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsection{Slope, Angle, and Vertical Change}

Take two distinct points on a non-vertical line. Let their horizontal and
vertical changes be
\[
\Delta x=x_2-x_1,
\qquad
\Delta y=y_2-y_1.
\]
Because the line is not vertical, $\Delta x\neq 0$.

\begin{definitionbox}
The \emph{slope} of a non-vertical line is
\[
\boxed{m=\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}}.
\]
It measures vertical change per unit of horizontal change.
\end{definitionbox}

For a line described by $y=mx+b$, replacing $x$ by $x+\Delta x$ changes $y$ by
\[
\Delta y=m\,\Delta x.
\]
Thus the coefficient $m$ in the equation is exactly the geometric slope.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.85]
    \draw[axis] (-0.8,0) -- (4.4,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-0.8) -- (0,6.0) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset,domain=-0.2:2.5,samples=2] plot (\x,{2*\x+1});
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (0.5,2) circle (2pt);
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (2,5) circle (2pt);
    \draw[helper] (0.5,2) -- (2,2) node[midway,below] {$\Delta x$};
    \draw[helper] (2,2) -- (2,5) node[midway,right] {$\Delta y$};
    \draw (1.15,2) arc[start angle=0,end angle=63.435,radius=0.65];
    \node at (1.35,2.35) {$\alpha$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The line and the positive horizontal direction form an angle $\alpha$. In the
right triangle shown above, the tangent of $\alpha$ is defined as
\[
\tan\alpha
=
\frac{\text{opposite side}}{\text{adjacent side}}
=
\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}.
\]

\begin{theorembox}
If $\alpha$ is an angle of inclination of a non-vertical line, then
\[
\boxed{m=\tan\alpha}.
\]
Thus slope is both a coordinate ratio and a description of direction.
\end{theorembox}

The sign of $m$ has a direct geometric meaning:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $m>0$: the line rises from left to right;
    \item $m<0$: the line falls from left to right;
    \item $m=0$: the line is horizontal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Constant Term and Axis Intersections}

For the line
\[
y=mx+b,
\]
setting $x=0$ gives $y=b$.

\begin{theorembox}
The line
\[
L_{m,b}=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=mx+b\}
\]
meets the $y$-axis at
\[
\boxed{(0,b)}.
\]
\end{theorembox}

To find an intersection with the $x$-axis, set $y=0$ and solve the resulting
equation for $x$.

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=-\tfrac12x+3\},
\]
the $y$-axis intersection is $(0,3)$. Setting $y=0$ gives
\[
0=-\frac12x+3,
\]
so $x=6$. Hence the $x$-axis intersection is $(6,0)$.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Vertical Lines}

A vertical line cannot be written as $y=mx+b$, because one value of $x$ is
paired with every real value of $y$.

\begin{definitionbox}
For a fixed $c\in\mathbb{R}$, the vertical line through $x=c$ is
\[
V_c=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x=c\}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.8]
    \draw[axis] (-4.0,0) -- (3.5,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-3.2) -- (0,3.5) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset] (-2,-3) -- (-2,3);
    \node[subset label,above] at (-2,3) {$V_{-2}$};
    \draw (-2,0.08) -- (-2,-0.08) node[below=3pt] {$-2$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{remarkbox}
A vertical line has no real-valued slope. For two distinct points on it,
$\Delta x=0$, so the quotient $\Delta y/\Delta x$ is undefined.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsection{The General Linear Equation}

\begin{definitionbox}
Let $a,b,c\in\mathbb{R}$, where $a$ and $b$ are not both zero. The set
\[
L_{a,b,c}=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:ax+by=c\}
\]
is a line in the Cartesian plane.
\end{definitionbox}

If $b\neq 0$, solving for $y$ gives
\[
y=-\frac{a}{b}x+\frac{c}{b}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{m=-\frac{a}{b}},
\qquad
\boxed{b_{\mathrm{intercept}}=\frac{c}{b}}.
\]
If $b=0$, then $a\neq 0$ and the condition becomes
\[
x=\frac{c}{a},
\]
which describes a vertical line.

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:2x+3y=6\},
\]
solving for $y$ gives
\[
y=-\frac23x+2.
\]
Thus the slope is $-2/3$, the $y$-axis intersection is $(0,2)$, and the
$x$-axis intersection is $(3,0)$.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{The Line Through Two Points}

Let
\[
P=(x_1,y_1),
\qquad
Q=(x_2,y_2)
\]
be distinct points.

\begin{theorembox}
If $x_1\neq x_2$, the unique line through $P$ and $Q$ has slope
\[
\boxed{m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}}
\]
and can be defined by
\[
\boxed{L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y-y_1=m(x-x_1)\}}.
\]
\end{theorembox}

The second formula follows because every point $(x,y)$ on the line must produce
the same vertical-to-horizontal change ratio from $P$.

If $x_1=x_2$, the two points determine the vertical line
\[
L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x=x_1\}.
\]

\begin{examplebox}
The points $P=(1,2)$ and $Q=(4,8)$ give
\[
m=\frac{8-2}{4-1}=2.
\]
Hence
\[
y-2=2(x-1),
\]
which simplifies to $y=2x$. Therefore
\[
L=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=2x\}.
\]
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Parallel Lines}

\begin{definitionbox}
Two distinct lines $L_1$ and $L_2$ are \emph{parallel} when they have no common
point:
\[
\boxed{L_1\neq L_2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
L_1\cap L_2=\varnothing.}
\]
\end{definitionbox}

Consider
\[
L_1=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=m_1x+b_1\},
\qquad
L_2=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=m_2x+b_2\}.
\]
A common point would have to satisfy
\[
m_1x+b_1=m_2x+b_2.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
(m_1-m_2)x=b_2-b_1.
\]
If $m_1\neq m_2$, this equation has exactly one solution for $x$, so the lines
intersect. If $m_1=m_2$, then either $b_1=b_2$ and the lines are identical, or
$b_1\neq b_2$ and no common point exists.

\begin{theorembox}
Two non-vertical lines are parallel precisely when
\[
\boxed{m_1=m_2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
b_1\neq b_2.}
\]
The equality of slopes is therefore a consequence of the geometric definition,
not the definition itself.
\end{theorembox}

Two distinct vertical lines are also parallel, because conditions $x=c_1$ and
$x=c_2$ with $c_1\neq c_2$ cannot hold at the same point.

\subsection{Perpendicular Lines}

\begin{definitionbox}
Two intersecting lines are \emph{perpendicular} when the angle formed at their
intersection is a right angle:
\[
\boxed{\frac{\pi}{2}}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

Let two non-vertical, non-horizontal lines have inclination angles $\alpha_1$
and $\alpha_2$. They are perpendicular exactly when their directions differ by
$\pi/2$. Thus we may write
\[
\alpha_2=\alpha_1+\frac{\pi}{2}
\]
up to reversing the direction along a line.

From the previously established relation $m=\tan\alpha$,
\[
m_1=\tan\alpha_1,
\qquad
m_2=\tan\alpha_2.
\]
Using
\[
\tan\left(\alpha+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)
=-\frac{1}{\tan\alpha},
\]
we obtain
\[
m_2=-\frac{1}{m_1}.
\]

\begin{theorembox}
For two non-vertical, non-horizontal lines,
\[
\boxed{L_1\perp L_2
\iff
m_1m_2=-1.}
\]
This criterion is derived from the geometric right-angle condition and the
relation $m=\tan\alpha$.
\end{theorembox}

A horizontal line and a vertical line form the remaining perpendicular case.
The product formula is not applicable there because the vertical line has no
real-valued slope.

\begin{remarkbox}
Two lines may intersect without being perpendicular. Intersection only says
that they have a common point. Perpendicularity additionally prescribes the
angle at that point.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{summarybox}
The central relations in this section are
\[
\begin{aligned}
L_{m,b}&=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=mx+b\},\\[2mm]
m&=\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}=\tan\alpha,\\[2mm]
L_1\parallel L_2&\iff m_1=m_2\text{ and }b_1\neq b_2,\\[2mm]
L_1\perp L_2&\iff m_1m_2=-1
\quad\text{(non-vertical, non-horizontal case).}
\end{aligned}
\]
Each algebraic criterion has been obtained from a geometric definition.
\end{summarybox}
