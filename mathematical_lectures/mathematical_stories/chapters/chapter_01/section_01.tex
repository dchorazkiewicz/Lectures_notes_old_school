\section{Points, Coordinates, and the Euclidean Plane}

Before using coordinates for geometry, we must distinguish three different
objects:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the set of ordered pairs of real numbers;
    \item a coordinate interpretation of those pairs;
    \item the additional rule that measures distance between pairs.
\end{enumerate}

These are related, but they are not the same thing.  In particular, the set
$\mathbb{R}^2$ by itself does not yet contain a notion of length, angle,
perpendicularity, or circle.

\subsection{The Set of Ordered Pairs}

\begin{definitionbox}
The Cartesian product of two copies of the real line is the set
\[
\mathbb{R}^2
=
\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}
=
\{(x,y):x\in\mathbb{R},\ y\in\mathbb{R}\}.
\]
Its elements are ordered pairs of real numbers.
\end{definitionbox}

The word \emph{ordered} is essential:
\[
(2,5)\neq(5,2).
\]
At this stage, however, an element $(x,y)$ is only an ordered pair.  The two
numbers could represent any two measured quantities.  Nothing in the set
$\mathbb{R}^2$ alone says that the first number is horizontal position, that the
second is vertical position, or that the two directions are perpendicular.

\begin{remarkbox}
The notation $\mathbb{R}^2$ specifies which objects are present, but not how
distances between them are measured.  A set and a geometry placed on that set
must not be confused.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsection{A Coordinate Interpretation}

To use $\mathbb{R}^2$ as a model of a plane, we choose two real number lines,
place one horizontally and the other vertically, and make their zero points
coincide.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-4.5,0) -- (4.7,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-3.5) -- (0,3.7) node[above] {$y$};

    \foreach \x in {-4,-3,-2,-1,1,2,3,4} {
        \draw (\x,0.08) -- (\x,-0.08) node[below=3pt] {\small $\x$};
    }
    \foreach \y in {-3,-2,-1,1,2,3} {
        \draw (0.08,\y) -- (-0.08,\y) node[left=3pt] {\small $\y$};
    }

    \node[below left=2pt] at (0,0) {$0$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The horizontal copy is called the $x$-axis and the vertical copy is called the
$y$-axis.  Their common zero is the origin,
\[
O=(0,0).
\]

\begin{definitionbox}
Under this coordinate interpretation, the ordered pair
\[
P=(x,y)
\]
labels the point reached by moving to the position $x$ along the horizontal
axis and to the position $y$ along the vertical axis.  The numbers $x$ and $y$
are called the coordinates of $P$.
\end{definitionbox}

This is an interpretation imposed on the ordered pairs.  It gives a
correspondence
\[
\text{coordinate point}
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\text{ordered pair in }\mathbb{R}^2.
\]
It still does not, by itself, define the distance between two points.

Consider
\[
P=(3,2).
\]
The coordinate construction places this point three units to the right of the
origin and two units above it.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-1.2,0) -- (5.0,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-1.2) -- (0,4.0) node[above] {$y$};

    \foreach \x in {-1,1,2,3,4} {
        \draw (\x,0.07) -- (\x,-0.07) node[below=3pt] {\small $\x$};
    }
    \foreach \y in {-1,1,2,3} {
        \draw (0.07,\y) -- (-0.07,\y) node[left=3pt] {\small $\y$};
    }

    \draw[helper] (3,0) -- (3,2);
    \draw[helper] (0,2) -- (3,2);
    \fill (3,2) circle (2.2pt);
    \node[above right] at (3,2) {$P=(3,2)$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The dashed lines are auxiliary.  They show how the two coordinates are read;
they are not part of the point.

\subsection{Distance: the Additional Geometric Structure}

Let
\[
P=(x_1,y_1),
\qquad
Q=(x_2,y_2).
\]
Their horizontal and vertical coordinate changes are
\[
\Delta x=x_2-x_1,
\qquad
\Delta y=y_2-y_1.
\]
The coordinate differences form the legs of a right triangle.  The
Pythagorean theorem therefore motivates the following rule.

\begin{definitionbox}
The Euclidean distance between $P=(x_1,y_1)$ and $Q=(x_2,y_2)$ is
\[
d_E(P,Q)
=
\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-0.8,0) -- (5.2,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-0.8) -- (0,4.5) node[above] {$y$};
    \coordinate (P) at (1,1);
    \coordinate (Q) at (4,3.5);
    \coordinate (R) at (4,1);
    \draw[subset] (P) -- (Q);
    \draw[helper] (P) -- (R) node[midway,below] {$|\Delta x|$};
    \draw[helper] (R) -- (Q) node[midway,right] {$|\Delta y|$};
    \draw (3.72,1) -- (3.72,1.28) -- (4,1.28);
    \fill (P) circle (2pt) node[below left] {$P$};
    \fill (Q) circle (2pt) node[above right] {$Q$};
    \node[subset label,above left] at (2.5,2.35) {$d_E(P,Q)$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The defining relation is
\[
\boxed{
 d_E(P,Q)^2
 =
 (\Delta x)^2+(\Delta y)^2
}.
\]

\begin{examplebox}
For $P=(0,0)$ and $Q=(3,4)$,
\[
d_E(P,Q)
=
\sqrt{3^2+4^2}
=5.
\]
\end{examplebox}

\begin{theorembox}
The function $d_E$ is a metric on $\mathbb{R}^2$.  For all
$P,Q,R\in\mathbb{R}^2$ it satisfies
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_E(P,Q)&\geq 0,\\
d_E(P,Q)=0&\iff P=Q,\\
d_E(P,Q)&=d_E(Q,P),\\
d_E(P,R)&\leq d_E(P,Q)+d_E(Q,R).
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{theorembox}

These properties formalise what is required of a distance.  The last one is
the triangle inequality.

\subsection{The Euclidean Plane}

\begin{definitionbox}
The standard Euclidean plane in coordinates is the metric space
\[
\boxed{\bigl(\mathbb{R}^2,d_E\bigr)}.
\]
It consists of the set $\mathbb{R}^2$ together with the Euclidean distance
$d_E$.
\end{definitionbox}

This distinction is essential:
\[
\boxed{
\mathbb{R}^2\text{ is a set of ordered pairs}
}
\qquad\text{whereas}\qquad
\boxed{
(\mathbb{R}^2,d_E)\text{ carries Euclidean geometry}.
}
\]

Lengths, metric circles, right angles, and perpendicularity depend on the
chosen geometric structure; they do not arise from the bare set of ordered
pairs.

\begin{remarkbox}
Other metrics can be placed on the same set $\mathbb{R}^2$.  For example,
\[
d_1(P,Q)=|x_2-x_1|+|y_2-y_1|
\]
and
\[
d_\infty(P,Q)
=
\max\{|x_2-x_1|,|y_2-y_1|\}.
\]
They measure distance differently and therefore produce different metric
circles.  This confirms that the geometry is additional structure, not a
consequence of the set $\mathbb{R}^2$ alone.
\end{remarkbox}

\subsection{Coordinate Conditions and Subsets}

Once the ambient set has been specified, a condition on $x$ and $y$ selects a
subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$.  For example,
\[
y=2
\]
is only a condition on two coordinates.  The corresponding subset is
\[
L=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=2\}.
\]

\begin{definitionbox}
The expression
\[
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:\mathcal{C}(x,y)\}
\]
denotes the set of all ordered pairs in $\mathbb{R}^2$ satisfying the condition
$\mathcal{C}(x,y)$.
\end{definitionbox}

The colon is read as ``such that.''  A vertical bar may be used instead:
\[
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=2\}
=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2\mid y=2\}.
\]

Under the coordinate interpretation, $L$ is a horizontal line.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.8]
    \draw[axis] (-4.2,0) -- (4.4,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-1.3) -- (0,3.8) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset] (-4,2) -- (4,2);
    \node[subset label,right] at (4,2) {$L$};
    \draw (0.08,2) -- (-0.08,2) node[left=3pt] {$2$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Similarly,
\[
M=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x=-1\}
\]
is a vertical line.

The coordinate axes themselves are subsets:
\[
X=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:y=0\},
\qquad
Y=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x=0\},
\]
and
\[
X\cap Y=\{(0,0)\}.
\]

\subsection{A Metric Circle}

A circle is not determined merely by the set $\mathbb{R}^2$.  It is defined
using a chosen distance.

\begin{definitionbox}
For a centre $O\in\mathbb{R}^2$ and radius $R>0$, the Euclidean circle with
centre $O$ and radius $R$ is
\[
C_E(O,R)
=
\{P\in\mathbb{R}^2:d_E(P,O)=R\}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

For $O=(0,0)$ and $R=2$, the distance condition is
\[
\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=2.
\]
Because both sides are non-negative, this is equivalent to
\[
x^2+y^2=4.
\]
Thus
\[
C_E(O,2)
=
\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x^2+y^2=4\}.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis] (-3.2,0) -- (3.4,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis] (0,-3.2) -- (0,3.4) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset] (0,0) circle (2);
    \fill (0,0) circle (1.7pt) node[below left] {$O$};
    \node[subset label,above right] at (1.4,1.4) {$C_E(O,2)$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{summarybox}
The logical order is
\[
\mathbb{R}^2
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{coordinate interpretation}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
d_E
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
(\mathbb{R}^2,d_E).
\]
The first object is a set.  The last object is the coordinate model of the
Euclidean plane.  Coordinate equations then define subsets of this ambient
space, and the metric gives geometric meaning to lengths and circles.
\end{summarybox}
