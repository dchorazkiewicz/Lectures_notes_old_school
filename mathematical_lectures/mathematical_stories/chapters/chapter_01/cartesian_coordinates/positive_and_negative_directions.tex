\subsection{Choosing Positive and Negative Directions}

\begin{derivationbox}[Choosing orientations]
Each axis is divided by $O$ into two opposite rays. Choose one ray on the
$x$-axis as positive and one ray on the $y$-axis as positive. The opposite rays
are then called negative.
\end{derivationbox}

\begin{corebox}[Orientation is a choice]
The Euclidean plane does not force which ray should be positive. Reversing one
orientation produces a different coordinate system on the same geometric
plane.
\end{corebox}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9]
    \draw[axis,->] (-4.2,0) -- (4.4,0) node[right] {positive $x$};
    \draw[axis,->] (0,-3.1) -- (0,3.3) node[above] {positive $y$};
    \fill (0,0) circle (2pt);
    \node[below left] at (0,0) {$O$};
    \node[below] at (-2.7,0) {negative $x$};
    \node[left] at (0,-2.2) {negative $y$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{interpretationbox}[What signs will mean]
A positive or negative coordinate will record on which oriented side of the
origin the corresponding projection lies. The sign is not a geometric length;
it is directional information supplied by the chosen orientation.
\end{interpretationbox}
