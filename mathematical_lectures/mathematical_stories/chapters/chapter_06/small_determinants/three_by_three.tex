\subsection{The Determinant of a $3\times3$ Matrix}

\begin{definitionbox}
For
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}
a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i
\end{pmatrix},
\]
define
\[
\boxed{\det A=aei+bfg+cdh-ceg-bdi-afh}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

Sarrus' rule is a mnemonic for this six-term formula: repeat the first two columns, add the three downward diagonal products, and subtract the three upward diagonal products.

\begin{remarkbox}[Limited scope of Sarrus' rule]
Sarrus' rule applies only to $3\times3$ determinants. It is not a method for size $4\times4$ or larger.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{examplebox}[Direct six-term calculation]
For
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}
1&2&0\\-1&3&4\\2&0&5
\end{pmatrix},
\]
\begin{align*}
\det A
&=(1\cdot3\cdot5+2\cdot4\cdot2+0\cdot(-1)\cdot0)\\
&\quad-(0\cdot3\cdot2+2\cdot(-1)\cdot5+1\cdot4\cdot0)\\
&=(15+16)-(0-10)=41.
\end{align*}
\end{examplebox}

\begin{examplebox}[Proportional rows]
For
\[
C=\begin{pmatrix}
1&2&0\\2&4&0\\3&1&5
\end{pmatrix},
\]
the formula gives $\det C=0$. The first two rows are proportional; later this cancellation becomes a general theorem.
\end{examplebox}
