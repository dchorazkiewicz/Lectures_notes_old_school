\subsection{Definition and the Two-Sided Condition}

For a nonzero number $a$, the inverse satisfies $aa^{-1}=1$. In matrix algebra the identity matrix replaces the number $1$.

\begin{definitionbox}
Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix. A matrix $B$ is an inverse of $A$ if
\[
AB=I_n
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
BA=I_n.
\]
If it exists, it is denoted by $A^{-1}$.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{corebox}[Both sides matter]
Matrix multiplication is not commutative. Therefore $AB=I$ and $BA=I$ are not merely two ways of writing the same calculation. The inverse must undo $A$ from both sides.
\end{corebox}

\begin{remarkbox}
The notation $A^{-1}$ does not mean $1/A$. It denotes the matrix that reverses the action of $A$, provided such a matrix exists.
\end{remarkbox}
