\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Determine whether
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\3&2\end{pmatrix}
\]
is invertible. If it is, compute $A^{-1}$ with the $2\times2$ formula and verify both products $AA^{-1}$ and $A^{-1}A$.

\item Compute the inverse of
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&3\\2&5\end{pmatrix}
\]
by Gauss--Jordan elimination, starting from $[A\mid I]$ and recording every row operation.

\item Compute the inverse of the diagonal matrix
\[
D=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&-4&0\\0&0&5\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Verify the result by multiplication.

\item Decide which matrices are invertible and compute the inverses when they exist:
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
C=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\item For
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\1&1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
b=\begin{pmatrix}5\\1\end{pmatrix},
\]
compute $A^{-1}$ and then solve $Ax=b$ using $x=A^{-1}b$.

\item Solve the matrix equation
\[
AX=B
\]
for
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&2\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\4&2\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\item Solve
\[
XA=B
\]
for the same matrices $A$ and $B$. Compare the answer with the previous exercise.

\item For
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix},
\]
write its cofactor matrix, its adjugate, and the expression
\[
\frac1{\det A}\operatorname{adj}(A).
\]
Then specialise the formula to $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&3\\1&4\end{pmatrix}$.

\item Let
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Compute $A^{-1}$, $B^{-1}$, $(AB)^{-1}$, and $B^{-1}A^{-1}$. Verify that the last two matrices agree.

\item During Gauss--Jordan elimination the left block becomes
\[
\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}
\]
instead of the identity. Explain what this means computationally, and confirm the conclusion by computing the determinant of the original matrix
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix}.
\]
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conceptual and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Explain the meaning of an inverse matrix as a reversal of an action. Why are both equations $AA^{-1}=I$ and $A^{-1}A=I$ important?

\item Show why a zero determinant prevents invertibility. Your explanation should connect determinant, loss of information, and failure of unique reversal.

\item Explain why the Gauss--Jordan construction
\[
[A\mid I]\longrightarrow[I\mid A^{-1}]
\]
works. Use elementary matrices or reversible row operations in your argument.

\item Why does increasing the dimension of a matrix make Gauss--Jordan longer but not conceptually different? Identify the steps that remain unchanged.

\item Compare the $2\times2$ inverse formula with Gauss--Jordan elimination. What idea does each method reveal most clearly?

\item Explain the structural content of
\[
A\operatorname{adj}(A)=(\det A)I.
\]
Why do cofactors produce diagonal entries equal to $\det A$ and off-diagonal entries equal to zero?

\item The adjugate method is usually inefficient for large matrices. Explain why it is still conceptually valuable in this course.

\item A student solves $AX=B$ by writing $X=BA^{-1}$. Explain the error and state the correct multiplication order. Then discuss why the order changes for $XA=B$.

\item Prove that if $A$ and $B$ are invertible, then
\[
(AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}.
\]
Explain why the order must reverse.

\item Give a coherent decision procedure for an inversion problem: how to test existence, which method to choose, how to verify the result, and how to interpret a failure.
\end{enumerate}
