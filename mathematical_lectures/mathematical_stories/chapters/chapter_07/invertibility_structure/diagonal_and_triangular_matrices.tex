\subsection{Diagonal and Triangular Matrices}

For
\[
D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\ldots,d_n),
\]
we have
\[
\det D=d_1\cdots d_n.
\]
Thus $D$ is invertible exactly when every $d_i$ is nonzero, and then
\[
\boxed{D^{-1}=\operatorname{diag}\left(\frac1{d_1},\ldots,\frac1{d_n}\right)}.
\]

\begin{examplebox}
For
\[
D=\begin{pmatrix}4&0&0\\0&-2&0\\0&0&5\end{pmatrix},
\]
\[
D^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}\frac14&0&0\\0&-\frac12&0\\0&0&\frac15\end{pmatrix}.
\]
\end{examplebox}

For a triangular matrix, the determinant is the product of the diagonal entries. Therefore it is invertible exactly when all diagonal entries are nonzero.

\begin{interpretationbox}[Pivots and reversibility]
A zero diagonal entry in triangular form means a missing pivot. Without one pivot in each coordinate direction, the matrix cannot be reduced to the identity.
\end{interpretationbox}
