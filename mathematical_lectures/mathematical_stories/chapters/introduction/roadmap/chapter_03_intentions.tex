\subsection*{Chapter 3: Lines and Planes in the Plane and in Space}

The third chapter uses the vector language to construct geometric sets. The
central question is no longer only what a vector is, but what collection of
points is created when motion is restricted to one or more independent
directions. Parameters, linear combinations, dot products, and cross products
now acquire direct geometric work to perform.

A major intention is to prevent equations from being mistaken for the objects
they describe. A line or plane is first constructed as a set of reachable points.
Its equations are then derived as alternative descriptions that reveal different
information.
