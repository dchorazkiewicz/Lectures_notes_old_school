\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item For
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}2&-1&4\\0&3&5\end{pmatrix},
\]
state its size and compute $a_{12}$, $a_{23}$, and the second row and third column as separate matrices.

\item Compute
\[
A+B,\qquad A-B,\qquad 3A
\]
for
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\-1&3\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}4&-2\\5&0\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\item Decide which of the following sums are defined, and compute those that are:
\[
\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}
+
\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\end{pmatrix}
+
\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\item Compute both products $AB$ and $BA$ for
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&-1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\4&2\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Compare the results.

\item Let
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1\\0&3&4\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
x=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Compute $Ax$ and write each entry as a row--column product.

\item Compute the transpose of
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&-2&0\\4&3&5\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then compute $(A^{\mathsf T})^{\mathsf T}$ and verify that it equals $A$.

\item For
\[
D=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&-1&0\\0&0&4\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
x=\begin{pmatrix}3\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix},
\]
compute $Dx$ and describe separately what happens to each coordinate.

\item Starting from
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1\\2&4&3\\0&1&5\end{pmatrix},
\]
perform
\[
R_2\leftarrow R_2-2R_1
\]
and write the resulting matrix. Then undo the operation explicitly.

\item Apply the following row operations in order to
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&7\end{pmatrix}:
\qquad
R_2\leftarrow R_2-3R_1,
\qquad
R_1\leftarrow R_1-2R_2.
\]
Record the matrix after each step.

\item Let
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},
\quad
B=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
\quad
C=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Compute $Ax$, $Bx$, and $Cx$ for $x=(4,-1)^{\mathsf T}$ and identify the visible action of each matrix.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conceptual and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Explain why the size of a matrix is part of its mathematical meaning and not merely a formatting detail.

\item The same rectangular array can represent data, coefficients of equations, or a transformation. Explain why the entries alone do not determine its role.

\item Why are matrix addition and scalar multiplication performed position by position, while matrix multiplication is not?

\item Derive the dimension rule
\[
(m\times n)(n\times p)=m\times p
\]
from the row--column definition of multiplication.

\item Give a concrete example showing that $AB\neq BA$. Explain why noncommutativity is natural when matrices are interpreted as successive actions.

\item Explain why the identity matrix behaves as a neutral action under multiplication. How is this different from the zero matrix?

\item A diagonal matrix is known before any multiplication is carried out. Explain what its visible pattern tells us about its action on coordinate vectors.

\item Why is a row operation more than arithmetic on entries? Describe its three layers: the calculation, the change of representation, and the relation that becomes visible.

\item Every elementary row operation is reversible. Explain why this reversibility is essential when row operations are later used for determinants, inverses, and systems of equations.

\item Compare a matrix row with a matrix column. Give one context in which rows naturally represent conditions and another in which columns naturally represent vectors or outputs.
\end{enumerate}
