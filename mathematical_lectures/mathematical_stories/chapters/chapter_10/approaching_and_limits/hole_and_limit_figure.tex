\subsection{A hole does not determine the nearby limit}

Consider the function that agrees with $y=x+1$ near $x=1$ but is not defined at $x=1$. The graph has a hole at $(1,2)$, yet the nearby values still approach $2$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[x=1.25cm,y=1.05cm]
  \draw[->] (-2.3,0) -- (3.1,0) node[right] {$x$};
  \draw[->] (0,-1.1) -- (0,4.3) node[above] {$y$};
  \draw[thick,domain=-1.8:2.6,samples=2] plot (\x,{\x+1});
  \fill[white] (1,2) circle (3pt);
  \draw[thick] (1,2) circle (3pt);
  \draw[dashed] (1,0) -- (1,2);
  \draw[dashed] (0,2) -- (1,2);
  \node[below] at (1,0) {$1$};
  \node[left] at (0,2) {$2$};
  \draw[->] (0.15,1.55) -- (0.82,1.93);
  \draw[->] (1.85,2.85) -- (1.18,2.12);
  \node at (-0.3,1.25) {from the left};
  \node at (2.2,3.15) {from the right};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{interpretationbox}[Value and approached value are different questions]
The missing point affects $f(1)$, but not the values taken for inputs close to $1$. Both sides of the graph approach the same height, so the limit is $2$ even though the function value at $1$ is absent.
\end{interpretationbox}
