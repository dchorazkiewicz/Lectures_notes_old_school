\section{Review Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item State the $\varepsilon$--$N$ definition of convergence of a sequence and explain the roles of $\varepsilon$, $N$, and the tail of the sequence.

\item Prove directly from the definition that $1/n\to0$. Which choice of $N$ is sufficient for a prescribed $\varepsilon>0$?

\item Explain the difference between a convergent sequence and a Cauchy sequence. Why does the reverse implication from Cauchy to convergence depend on completeness of $\mathbb R$?

\item State the monotone convergence theorem. Explain why monotonicity and boundedness work together, and give examples showing that either property alone is insufficient.

\item State the $\varepsilon$--$\delta$ definition of
\[
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L.
\]
Use the diagrammatic language of the input interval and output band, and explain why $x=a$ is excluded.

\item State Heine's theorem. Explain why one approaching sequence can disprove a proposed limit but cannot by itself prove the existence of the limit.

\item Compare the ordinary limit definition, Heine's theorem, and the Cauchy criterion for a function limit. What quantities are compared in each formulation?

\item Determine the following elementary limits and explain the method chosen in each case:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a limit evaluated by direct substitution;
\item a removable $0/0$ form resolved by factorisation;
\item a radical limit resolved with a conjugate;
\item a rational-function limit at infinity using dominant powers.
\end{enumerate}

\item Explain the difference among a two-sided limit, one-sided limits, and an infinite limit. How are vertical and horizontal asymptotes expressed as limit statements?

\item Define continuity at a point and classify removable, jump, and infinite discontinuities. Then state the intermediate value theorem and explain what it proves about roots without actually finding them.
\end{enumerate}
