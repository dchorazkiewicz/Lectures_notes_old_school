\subsection{Classification before formulas}

\begin{corebox}[First 45 minutes: conditions, geometry, and elimination]
The essential problem is to describe the complete solution set. Geometry explains the three possible outcomes, while Gaussian elimination is the general method that detects one solution, inconsistency, or free variables.
\end{corebox}

\begin{corebox}[Second 45 minutes: special square-system viewpoints]
For square systems with nonzero determinant, Cramer's rule and the inverse formula provide elegant alternative descriptions. They are not replacements for elimination: both require invertibility and neither classifies singular systems by itself. Parameter problems return to elimination and compatibility.
\end{corebox}

The hierarchy is therefore deliberate: elimination is the principal method; inverse matrices explain reversal; Cramer's rule displays the determinant connection.
