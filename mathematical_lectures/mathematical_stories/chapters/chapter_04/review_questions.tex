\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational and graphical exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Find the centre and radius of
\[
(x-2)^2+(y+3)^2=25.
\]
Sketch the circle and mark the four points directly above, below, left, and right of the centre.

\item Rewrite
\[
x^2+y^2-6x+4y-3=0
\]
in standard circle form. State the centre and radius.

\item A parabola has focus $F=(0,2)$ and directrix $y=-2$. Derive its equation from the equal-distance condition and identify the vertex and opening direction.

\item For
\[
x^2=12y,
\]
find $p$, the focus, the directrix, and the vertex. Check the equal-distance condition at $(6,3)$.

\item For the ellipse
\[
\frac{x^2}{25}+\frac{y^2}{9}=1,
\]
find the semiaxes, vertices, foci, and $c$. Sketch the curve.

\item Rewrite
\[
9x^2+4y^2-36x+8y+4=0
\]
in standard ellipse form and find its centre and semiaxes.

\item For the hyperbola
\[
\frac{x^2}{16}-\frac{y^2}{9}=1,
\]
find the vertices, foci, and asymptotes. Sketch both branches.

\item Rewrite
\[
4x^2-9y^2-8x-18y-41=0
\]
in standard hyperbola form and identify the centre and transverse direction.

\item Classify and sketch each curve from the signs and missing terms:
\[
x^2+4y^2=4,
\qquad
x^2-y^2=1,
\qquad
x^2=4y.
\]
State the decisive feature in each equation.

\item Find the intersections of
\[
y=x^2
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
y=2x+3.
\]
Plot both curves and verify every intersection in both equations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Development and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Starting from the fixed-distance definition, derive the equation of a circle with centre $(h,k)$ and radius $r$. Then specialise it to a concrete circle and compare the geometric and algebraic descriptions.

\item Starting from the focus-directrix definition, derive the equation of the parabola with focus $(p,0)$ and directrix $x=-p$. Explain why the equation contains only one squared variable.

\item An ellipse has foci $(-3,0)$ and $(3,0)$ and constant sum of distances $10$. Determine its standard equation, vertices, and minor semiaxis. Explain where the condition $10>6$ is used.

\item A hyperbola has foci $(-5,0)$ and $(5,0)$ and constant absolute difference of distances $6$. Determine its standard equation and asymptotes, and explain why two branches appear.

\item Rewrite and classify
\[
4x^2+9y^2-16x+18y-11=0.
\]
Show every completing-square step and explain which geometric information becomes visible after each translation.

\item Compare the curves
\[
x^2+y^2=0,
\qquad
x^2-y^2=0,
\qquad
x^2+y^2=-1.
\]
Classify the degenerate or empty cases and explain why a second-degree equation need not produce a nondegenerate conic.

\item The equation
\[
x^2+4xy+4y^2=1
\]
contains a mixed term. Factor its quadratic part, describe the geometry suggested by the factorisation, and explain why a rotation or new coordinate direction is natural.

\item For
\[
z=x^2+y^2,
\]
compute and sketch the sections $x=0$, $y=0$, and $z=c$ for several positive values of $c$. Use the sections to justify the name elliptic paraboloid.

\item For
\[
z=x^2-y^2,
\]
compute the sections $x=0$, $y=0$, and $z=0$. Explain how the different section types reveal saddle behaviour.

\item Explain the two complementary tasks of the chapter: constructing a conic from a geometric condition and recognising geometry hidden in an equation. Use one complete example of each and compare the reasoning required.
\end{enumerate}
