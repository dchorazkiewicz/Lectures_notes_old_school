\section{Review Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Explain why quadratic terms arise naturally when Euclidean distance conditions are translated into Cartesian coordinates.

\item Define a circle geometrically and derive its standard Cartesian equation. Which parts of the equation record the centre and radius?

\item Define a parabola using a focus and a directrix. Derive the equation $x^2=4py$ for the standard placement and interpret the parameter $p$.

\item Compare the geometric definitions of an ellipse and a hyperbola. Why does a fixed sum produce a bounded curve while a fixed difference produces two unbounded branches?

\item For
\[
\frac{x^2}{9}+\frac{y^2}{4}=1,
\]
identify the curve, its centre, semiaxes, vertices, and foci. Explain which facts come directly from the equation and which use the geometric definition.

\item For
\[
\frac{x^2}{4}-\frac{y^2}{5}=1,
\]
identify the curve, its transverse direction, vertices, foci, and asymptotic behaviour.

\item Explain how translations and completing the square reveal the geometry hidden in a general second-degree equation. Work out the centre and radius of
\[
x^2+y^2-4x+6y-3=0.
\]

\item What is the role of a coordinate rotation in the classification of a quadratic equation containing an $xy$ term? What geometric object remains unchanged during the rotation?

\item Explain how circles, ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas arise as planar sections of a double cone. What changes as the cutting plane is tilted?

\item Describe how planar sections can be used to recognise a quadric surface. For a proposed surface, explain what information is obtained from cuts by $x=c$, $y=c$, and $z=c$.
\end{enumerate}
