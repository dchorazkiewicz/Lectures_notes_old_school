\section{Review Exercises}

\subsection{Elementary computational and construction exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Find the centre and radius of
\[
(x-2)^2+(y+3)^2=25.
\]
Plot the centre and the four points where the circle meets the horizontal and vertical lines through its centre.

\item Rewrite
\[
x^2+y^2-6x+4y-3=0
\]
in standard circle form by completing squares. State the centre and radius.

\item A parabola has focus $F=(0,2)$ and directrix $y=-2$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item derive its Cartesian equation from the equal-distance condition;
\item identify its vertex and direction of opening.
\end{enumerate}

\item For the parabola
\[
x^2=12y,
\]
find the value of $p$, the focus, and the directrix. Check that the point $(6,3)$ satisfies the equal-distance condition.

\item For the ellipse
\[
\frac{x^2}{25}+\frac{y^2}{9}=1,
\]
find the semiaxes, vertices, foci, and the value of $c$. Sketch the curve.

\item Rewrite
\[
9x^2+4y^2-36x+8y+4=0
\]
in standard ellipse form. Find its centre and semiaxes.

\item For the hyperbola
\[
\frac{x^2}{16}-\frac{y^2}{9}=1,
\]
find the vertices, foci, and asymptotes. Sketch both branches.

\item Rewrite
\[
4x^2-9y^2-8x-18y-41=0
\]
in standard hyperbola form and identify its centre and transverse direction.

\item Identify each surface and describe three useful coordinate-plane sections:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $x^2+y^2+z^2=9$;
\item $z=x^2+y^2$;
\item $x^2+y^2-z^2=1$.
\end{enumerate}

\item For the surface
\[
z=x^2-y^2,
\]
compute and sketch the sections obtained from $x=0$, $y=0$, and $z=0$. Use them to make a rough sketch of the surface.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conceptual and reasoning exercises}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Explain why quadratic terms arise naturally when Euclidean distance conditions are translated into Cartesian coordinates.

\item Distinguish a geometric definition of a conic from its standard Cartesian equation. Why should the equation be regarded as a representation rather than the object itself?

\item Starting from the fixed-distance definition of a circle, derive its standard equation and identify exactly where the Pythagorean theorem enters.

\item Explain geometrically why a parabola has one branch and is unbounded even though its points remain equally distant from a fixed point and a fixed line.

\item The ellipse is defined by a fixed sum of distances to two foci. Explain why this condition forces boundedness. What would fail if the prescribed sum were not larger than the distance between the foci?

\item The hyperbola replaces a fixed sum by a fixed difference. Explain why this change creates two unbounded branches.

\item Describe how circle, ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola arise from one experiment of cutting a double cone with planes of different inclinations. Why is this more informative than memorising four unrelated formulas?

\item Completing squares may translate the centre of a conic. Explain what changes in the equation and what geometric object remains unchanged.

\item A second-degree equation contains an $xy$ term. Explain why rotating the coordinate axes may reveal a standard conic. What changes under the rotation: the curve, the coordinates, or both?

\item Explain why planar sections are an effective way to understand a quadric surface. Give an example where different sections reveal boundedness, disconnected components, or saddle behaviour.
\end{enumerate}
