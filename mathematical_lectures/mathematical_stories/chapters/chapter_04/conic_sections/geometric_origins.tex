\section{Conic Sections: From Geometric Conditions to Equations}

The previous chapter studied sets determined by first-degree equations. We now
ask what curves arise when distances between moving and fixed geometric objects
are constrained.

\begin{corebox}[Why quadratic equations appear]
Euclidean distance contains squares. When a geometric distance condition is
translated into Cartesian coordinates and square roots are removed,
second-degree terms arise naturally. Quadratic equations are consequences of
geometry, not an arbitrary next list of formulas.
\end{corebox}

\subsection{A Circle: One Fixed Point and One Fixed Distance}

\begin{definitionbox}
Fix a centre $C=(h,k)$ and a number $r>0$. The circle with centre $C$ and radius
$r$ is the set of points $P=(x,y)$ satisfying
\[
\boxed{d(P,C)=r}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{derivationbox}[Cartesian equation of the circle]
Using the Euclidean distance formula,
\[
\sqrt{(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2}=r.
\]
Both sides are nonnegative, so squaring preserves the condition and gives
\[
\boxed{(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2}.
\]
\end{derivationbox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[Definition versus equation]
The equation is not the definition of the circle. It is the Cartesian expression
of the geometric requirement that every point remain a fixed distance from one
centre.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{A Parabola: Equal Distance from a Point and a Line}

\begin{definitionbox}
Fix a point $F$, called the focus, and a line $D$, called the directrix. A
parabola is the set of points $P$ satisfying
\[
\boxed{d(P,F)=d(P,D)}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

Choose coordinates so that
\[
F=(0,p),
\qquad
D:\ y=-p,
\qquad p>0.
\]

\begin{derivationbox}[Cartesian equation of the parabola]
For $P=(x,y)$,
\[
d(P,F)=\sqrt{x^2+(y-p)^2},
\qquad
d(P,D)=|y+p|.
\]
On the resulting parabola $y\geq-p$, so $|y+p|=y+p$. Hence
\[
\sqrt{x^2+(y-p)^2}=y+p.
\]
Squaring and cancelling equal terms gives
\[
x^2+y^2-2py+p^2=y^2+2py+p^2,
\]
and therefore
\[
\boxed{x^2=4py}.
\]
\end{derivationbox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[What the parabola equation records]
The equation $x^2=4py$ is the Cartesian form of the equal-distance condition.
It should always be read together with the focus and directrix geometry that
produced it.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{An Ellipse: A Fixed Sum of Two Distances}

\begin{definitionbox}
Fix two foci
\[
F_1=(-c,0),\qquad F_2=(c,0),\qquad c>0,
\]
and choose $a>c$. An ellipse is the set of points $P$ satisfying
\[
\boxed{d(P,F_1)+d(P,F_2)=2a}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{remarkbox}
The inequality $a>c$ is necessary: the prescribed sum must exceed the distance
$2c$ between the foci.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{derivationbox}[Cartesian equation of the ellipse]
Write
\[
r_1=\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2},
\qquad
r_2=\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}.
\]
The condition is $r_1+r_2=2a$. Isolating and squaring twice gives
\[
\left(1-\frac{c^2}{a^2}\right)x^2+y^2=a^2-c^2.
\]
Define
\[
b^2=a^2-c^2>0.
\]
Then
\[
\boxed{
\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1
}.
\]
\end{derivationbox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[Why the ellipse is bounded]
The total journey from one focus to the moving point and then to the other focus
is fixed. This constraint prevents the point from moving arbitrarily far away
and produces a bounded oval.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{A Hyperbola: A Fixed Difference of Two Distances}

\begin{definitionbox}
With the same foci, choose $0<a<c$. A hyperbola is the set of points $P$
satisfying
\[
\boxed{|d(P,F_1)-d(P,F_2)|=2a}.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{derivationbox}[Cartesian equation of the hyperbola]
On the right branch, write $r_1-r_2=2a$. Isolating the remaining square root and
squaring twice gives
\[
\left(\frac{c^2}{a^2}-1\right)x^2-y^2=c^2-a^2.
\]
Set
\[
b^2=c^2-a^2>0.
\]
Then
\[
\boxed{
\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1
}.
\]
\end{derivationbox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[Why two branches appear]
The absolute value allows either focus to be nearer. Changing a fixed sum into a
fixed difference changes a bounded curve into two unbounded branches. The new
geometry comes from changing the distance condition, not from choosing an
unrelated formula.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{Why These Curves Form One Family}

\begin{corebox}[One spatial experiment]
Intersect a double cone with a plane and vary the inclination of the cutting
plane. A perpendicular cut gives a circle; a tilted cut meeting one nappe gives
an ellipse; the limiting parallel cut gives a parabola; and a cut meeting both
nappes gives a hyperbola.
\end{corebox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[The unity of conic sections]
The four curves are not an arbitrary list. They arise by varying one geometric
operation while keeping the cone fixed. Their different equations record
different outcomes of the same spatial construction.
\end{interpretationbox}

\begin{remarkbox}
The rest of the chapter studies how these geometric sets appear in Cartesian
equations. Standard forms, translations, rotations, and discriminants are tools
for recognising the object already present in an equation. They do not replace
the geometric definition of the curve.
\end{remarkbox}

\begin{summarybox}[Geometric definition before algebraic recognition]
A circle fixes one distance, a parabola equates two distances, an ellipse fixes
a sum, and a hyperbola fixes a difference. In each case the quadratic equation
is derived from the geometric condition and should be interpreted through it.
\end{summarybox}
