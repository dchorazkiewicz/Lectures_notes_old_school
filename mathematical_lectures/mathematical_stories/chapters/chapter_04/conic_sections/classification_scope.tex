\subsection{From Construction to Recognition}

The geometric definitions answer the question \emph{what the curves are}. A
different problem begins when an equation is given first.

\begin{corebox}[The recognition question]
Which geometric curve, if any, is hidden in a given second-degree algebraic
condition?
\end{corebox}

\begin{corebox}[Two different tasks]
Constructing a conic starts from a geometric condition and derives an equation.
Recognising a conic starts from an equation and reconstructs the geometry.
Standard forms, completing squares, translations, rotations, and discriminants
belong to the second task; they do not replace the geometric definition.
\end{corebox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[First visual clues in the equation]
When no mixed term is present, squared terms with the same sign suggest an
ellipse-type set, opposite signs suggest hyperbolic behaviour, and the absence
of one quadratic direction suggests a parabola. Degenerate cases may instead
produce points, lines, or no real points.
\end{interpretationbox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[What the mixed term means]
A term $Bxy$ indicates that the chosen coordinate axes may not follow the
natural symmetry directions of the curve. Rotating the coordinate system can
remove the mixed term and reveal the standard geometry.
\end{interpretationbox}

\begin{remarkbox}
This subsection motivates the recognition problem. The precise classification
criterion and its exceptional cases are stated once in the algebraic
classification that follows.
\end{remarkbox}
