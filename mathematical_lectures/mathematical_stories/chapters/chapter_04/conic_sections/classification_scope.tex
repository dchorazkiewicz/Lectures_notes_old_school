\section{Polar Coordinates and One Equation for the Conics}

Cartesian coordinates ask for horizontal and vertical displacement. When one
focus is distinguished, a different pair of questions becomes natural:

\begin{center}
\emph{In which direction is the point, and how far is it from the focus?}
\end{center}

\subsection{The polar coordinate system}

Choose a point $O$, called the pole, and a ray from it, called the polar axis. A
point $P\neq O$ is described by
\[
\boxed{(r,\theta)},
\]
where $r=d(O,P)>0$ and $\theta$ is the directed angle from the polar axis to the
ray $OP$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9,>=stealth]
    \draw[axis,->] (-0.6,0)--(6.0,0) node[right] {polar axis};
    \coordinate (O) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (P) at ({4.6*cos(34)},{4.6*sin(34)});
    \draw[very thick,->] (O)--(P) node[midway,above left] {$r$};
    \draw (1.2,0) arc[start angle=0,end angle=34,radius=1.2];
    \node at (1.45,0.48) {$\theta$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (O) circle (2.2pt) node[below left] {$O$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (P) circle (2.2pt) node[above right] {$P$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The conversion between Cartesian and polar coordinates is
\[
\boxed{x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta}.
\]
Conversely,
\[
\boxed{r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}},
\]
and the angle is chosen so that
\[
\cos\theta=\frac xr,
\qquad
\sin\theta=\frac yr.
\]

\begin{interpretationbox}[Coordinate curves]
In Cartesian coordinates, $x=\text{constant}$ and $y=\text{constant}$ are lines.
In polar coordinates, $r=\text{constant}$ is a circle centred at the pole, while
$\theta=\text{constant}$ is a ray. The coordinate system is adapted to distance
and direction from one distinguished point.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{A first polar graph}

A circle centred at the pole has the exceptionally simple equation
\[
\boxed{r=R}.
\]
Every direction meets the circle at the same distance.

A shifted circle already shows why polar equations can encode geometry in a
compact way. Consider
\[
r=2R\cos\theta.
\]
Multiplying by $r$ and using $x=r\cos\theta$ gives
\[
r^2=2Rx,
\]
so
\[
x^2+y^2=2Rx,
\]
or
\[
\boxed{(x-R)^2+y^2=R^2}.
\]
Thus the polar graph is a circle of radius $R$ centred at $(R,0)$ and passing
through the pole.

    \draw[axis,->] (0,-2.8)--(0,2.8) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[subset] (2.0,0) circle (2.0);
    \draw[very thick,->] (0,0)--({4*cos(42)*cos(42)},{4*cos(42)*sin(42)})
        node[midway,above left] {$r=2R\cos\theta$};
    \draw (0.8,0) arc[start angle=0,end angle=42,radius=0.8];
    \node at (1.05,0.35) {$\theta$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (0,0) circle (2pt) node[below left] {pole};

\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\subsection{Focus, directrix, and eccentricity}

Fix a focus $F$ and a line $D$ not passing through it. For a number $e>0$,
consider all points $P$ satisfying
\[
\boxed{d(P,F)=e\,d(P,D)}.
\]
The number $e$ is called the \emph{eccentricity}.

\begin{theorembox}
The focus--directrix condition produces
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
0<e<1 & \text{ellipse},\\
e=1 & \text{parabola},\\
e>1 & \text{hyperbola}.
\end{array}
\]
A circle is the limiting ellipse with eccentricity $e=0$.
\end{theorembox}

\begin{interpretationbox}[What eccentricity measures]
Eccentricity measures departure from circular symmetry. Small $e$ gives an
almost circular ellipse. As $e$ approaches $1$, the ellipse becomes more
elongated. At $e=1$ the curve opens into a parabola; for $e>1$ it becomes a
hyperbola.
\end{interpretationbox}

\subsection{Deriving the common polar equation}

Place the pole at the focus $F=(0,0)$ and let the directrix be the vertical line
\[
x=d,
\qquad d>0.
\]
For a point $P=(r,\theta)$, its distance from the focus is $r$. Its Cartesian
horizontal coordinate is $r\cos\theta$, so its distance from the directrix is
\[
d-r\cos\theta
\]
for the branch lying to the left of the directrix.

The focus--directrix condition becomes
\[
r=e(d-r\cos\theta).
\]
Therefore
\[
r+er\cos\theta=ed,
\]
and hence
\[
\boxed{r(1+e\cos\theta)=ed}.
\]
Introduce the focal parameter
\[
\boxed{p=ed}.
\]
Then all three conics are described by
\[
\boxed{r(\theta)=\frac{p}{1+e\cos\theta}}.
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9,>=stealth]
    \draw[axis,->] (-1.2,0)--(7.0,0) node[right] {$x$};
    \draw[axis,->] (0,-3.3)--(0,3.4) node[above] {$y$};
    \draw[gray,thick] (5.8,-3.0)--(5.8,3.0) node[above] {$D:x=d$};
    \coordinate (F) at (0,0);
    \coordinate (P) at ({3.7*cos(32)},{3.7*sin(32)});
    \coordinate (H) at (5.8,{3.7*sin(32)});
    \draw[very thick,->] (F)--(P) node[midway,above left] {$r$};
    \draw[orange!85!black,line width=1.1pt] (P)--(H)
        node[midway,above] {$d-r\cos\theta$};
    \draw (1.0,0) arc[start angle=0,end angle=32,radius=1.0];
    \node at (1.25,0.38) {$\theta$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (F) circle (2.2pt) node[below left] {$F$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (P) circle (2.2pt) node[above] {$P$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{corebox}[One formula, three geometries]
The equation
\[
r=\frac{p}{1+e\cos\theta}
\]
is not a new definition detached from the classical curves. It is the
focus--directrix condition written in coordinates centred at the focus.
\end{corebox}

\subsection{Reading the geometry directly from the denominator}

At $\theta=0$,
\[
\boxed{r_{\min}=\frac{p}{1+e}}.
\]
For an ellipse, at $\theta=\pi$,
\[
\boxed{r_{\max}=\frac{p}{1-e}}.
\]
Thus the same equation immediately identifies the nearest and farthest points
from the focus.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.88,>=stealth]
    \draw[axis,->] (-4.7,0)--(5.2,0) node[right] {polar axis};
    \draw[axis,->] (0,-3.0)--(0,3.2);
    \draw[subset] (0.7,0) ellipse (3.6 and 2.1);
    \coordinate (F) at (-1.9,0);

    \coordinate (B) at (4.3,0);
    \draw[blue!70!black,line width=1.1pt,<->] (F)--(A)
        node[midway,below] {$r_{\min}$};
    \draw[orange!85!black,line width=1.1pt,<->] (F)--(B)
        node[midway,above] {$r_{\max}$};
    \fill[geometricSubsetColor] (F) circle (2.2pt) node[below] {focus};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\begin{summarybox}[Why polar coordinates matter]
Cartesian coordinates display axes, centres, vertices, and algebraic form.
Polar coordinates centred at a focus display direction, focal distance, and
eccentricity. The two descriptions represent the same curve but expose
different geometric information.
\end{summarybox}
