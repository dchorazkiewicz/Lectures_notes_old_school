\chapter{Conic Sections and Quadric Surfaces}

\begin{corebox}[Purpose and learning goals]
This chapter connects geometric definitions of conics with their quadratic equations and extends the same viewpoint to surfaces. After completing it, the student should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
\item recognise circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas from geometry and equations;
\item use completing squares and coordinate changes to expose geometric structure;
\item identify basic quadric surfaces from their planar sections.
\end{itemize}
\end{corebox}

\begin{summarybox}[From curves to surfaces]
A circle, parabola, ellipse, or hyperbola is first determined by a geometric
condition and then represented by an equation. Completing squares and rotating
coordinates recover that geometry from a general quadratic equation. In three
dimensions, families of planar sections reveal whether a quadric is bounded,
connected, separated, cylindrical, conical, or saddle-shaped.
\end{summarybox}
